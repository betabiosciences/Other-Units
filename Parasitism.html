PARASITISM

Introduction to Parasitism

Parasitism is a type of symbiotic relationship between organisms of different species in which the parasite, benefits from a prolonged, close association with the other, the host, which is harmed. Usually, parasites are much smaller than their hosts, show a high degree of specialization for their mode of life and reproduce more quickly and in greater numbers than their hosts. Classic examples of parasitism include the interactions between vertebrate hosts and such diverse animals as the tapeworms, flukes, Plasmodium species and fleas.

The detriment and benefit in parasitic interactions concern the biological fitness of the organisms involved. Parasites reduce host fitness in many ways, ranging from general or specialized pathology (such as castration), impairment of secondary sex characteristics, to the modification of host behaviour. Parasites increase their fitness by exploiting hosts for food, habitat and dispersal.

Types of Parasitism

Microparasites

These are tiny parasites, like pathogenic bacteria and viruses, that live and multiply directly inside their hosts, often within the host's cells. They reproduce rapidly, and their infections are typically described as diseases. Transmission can happen directly between hosts or be aided by another organism, like a mosquito. The virulence of a microparasite refers to how much it harms or kills its host. Hosts generally have a strong and immediate immune response to these infections.

Macroparasites

These parasites are usually visible to the naked eye and have a longer generation time. Examples include schistosomes. They live inside a host but release their infective young stages outside of it. Most macroparasites need more than one host species to complete their life cycle. A host's immune response to these infections is usually weak and temporary, leading to long-lasting infections and the possibility of getting infected again and again.

Parasitoids

Parasitoids are parasitic insects that lay their eggs in or on another insect's immature stage (like a larva or pupa). The parasitoid larvae then develop inside the host, eventually killing it when the adult emerges. This interaction is similar to predation because the host is ultimately killed. If a parasitoid is itself a host for another parasitoid, the second one is called a hyperparasitoid.

Ectoparasites and Endoparasites

This classification is based on where the parasite lives on the host. Ectoparasites, such as ticks and fleas, live on the outside of the host's body. Endoparasites, like pathogenic bacteria and tapeworms, live inside the host. Endoparasitism requires more specialized adaptations to overcome the host's defenses. This is why ectoparasites like leeches are often polyphagous (having many hosts), while endoparasites like liver flukes are more likely to be monophagous (having only one host).

The text notes that some plant parasites, like mistletoe, can be both ecto- and endoparasitic. They have parts that are outside the host and other parts, called haustoria, that penetrate inside to get nutrients.

Plant Parasites

Holoparasites and Hemiparasites

This category further divides parasitic flowering plants. Holoparasites are entirely dependent on their host for water and nutrients because they lack chlorophyll and cannot photosynthesize. A notable example is the massive flower of Rafflesia arnoldii, which lives most of its life inside a vine host. These parasites often have a very limited range of hosts.

Hemiparasites, on the other hand, can photosynthesize but lack a root system and must draw water from their host. Mistletoe is a good example. Hemiparasites typically have a broader range of hosts than holoparasites do.

Enslaver Parasites

Many macroparasites have complex life cycles, meaning they need at least two different host species to complete their life cycle. A definitive host is where the parasite sexually reproduces, while an intermediate host contains the parasite's non-reproducing forms. Many of these parasites, often called "enslaver parasites," manipulate their intermediate hosts to increase the likelihood of being transmitted to the definitive host.

Enslaver parasites are parasites that manipulate the behavior or physical appearance of their host to increase the chances of the parasite being transmitted to its next host in a complex life cycle. This manipulation often makes the infected host more vulnerable to predation by the definitive host.

Parasitic Manipulation of Host Behavior

Parasites can change the behavior, color, or other characteristics of their intermediate host to make it more vulnerable to being eaten by the definitive host. This manipulation increases the chances of the parasite completing its life cycle.

  • Pillbugs and Thorny-headed Worms: The parasitic worm Plagiorhynchus cylindraceus lives in songbirds. Its eggs are consumed by pillbugs, which are an intermediate host. The parasites alter the pillbugs' behavior, causing them to move away from their usual humid, hidden spots into open areas where they are more easily seen and eaten by birds.
  • Ants and Lancet Flukes: The lancet fluke, Dicrocoelium dendritium, reproduces in cows. Its eggs are consumed by snails, which produce slime balls full of immature flukes. Ants then eat these slime balls, and the flukes move to the ant's head, causing it to climb to the top of grass blades. This behavior makes the ant more likely to be eaten by a grazing cow, continuing the cycle.

More Manipulation Examples

  • Amphipods and Acanthocephalans: The freshwater amphipod Gammarus pulex can be infected by two different acanthocephalan species. Uninfected amphipods hide from predators by staying near the bottom of the water column. However, those infected with Pomphorhynchus laevis are attracted to light, making them swim out in the open and be eaten by fish. Similarly, those infected with Polymorphus minutus swim near the surface, increasing their chances of being eaten by birds.
  • Rats and Toxoplasma gondii: Toxoplasma gondii can infect many mammals but only reproduces in cats. It encysts in the brains of intermediate hosts, like rats. Normally, rats avoid the smell of cats, but infected rats are actually attracted to cat urine, which makes them more likely to be preyed upon.

Parasitic Manipulation of Feeding Behavior

Some parasites manipulate the feeding behavior of their vectors (organisms that transmit the parasite). This manipulation increases the number of bites or feeding attempts, thereby maximizing the transmission of the parasite to new hosts.

  • Malaria and Mosquitoes: The malaria parasite, Plasmodium, reproduces in mosquitoes and vertebrates. It interferes with a mosquito's ability to draw blood efficiently. This makes the mosquito bite more frequently and on multiple hosts, increasing the rate at which the parasite is transmitted. This behavior is also seen in other parasites like leishmaniasis in sand flies and bubonic plague in fleas.

Kleptoparasitism

Kleptoparasitism is a type of feeding where one animal steals food or resources that another has already acquired. This behavior can occur between members of the same species or different species. For example, some small spiders steal prey from the webs of larger spiders, while some seagulls and frigatebirds snatch food from other birds that have just caught it.

Brood Parasitism

Brood parasitism is a specific form of kleptoparasitism where one species manipulates another to raise its young. In this scenario, the parasitic parent lays its eggs in the nest of a host species, leaving the host to incubate the eggs and care for the hatchlings. Common examples include cuckoos, cowbirds, and certain other bird species.

Brood Parasitism Details

  • Why Hosts Allow It: You might wonder why host birds don't just get rid of the foreign eggs. While some hosts can recognize and eject parasitic eggs, the "mafia hypothesis" suggests a reason for their compliance. This theory proposes that parasitic birds like cuckoos and cowbirds will monitor the nests they've targeted. If they find their egg has been removed, they may destroy the host's nest, and harm or kill the host's young. This forces the host to build a new nest, giving the parasite another opportunity to lay an egg.
  • Defenses: The best way for host birds to defend against this is to prevent the parasitism from happening in the first place. They might choose nesting sites that are hard for parasites to find or access, or nest in large groups to provide a collective defense.

Brood Parasitism Specialization

  • Specialization: Although some brood parasites use many different host species, individual females often specialize on just one or a few. It's believed that female cuckoos, for example, learn to target the species that raised them. They can also pass on genes for egg color through the maternal line, which helps them lay eggs that mimic the appearance of the host's eggs. Meanwhile, male cuckoos mate with females from all different host lines, ensuring genetic diversity.

Host Defenses against Parasites

Hosts have developed many defenses against parasites, which can be behavioral or part of their immune system.

Immune System Defenses

All organisms possess an immune system with two main components:

  • Innate Immunity: These are the body's natural defenses that are present from birth. They act as a general, non-specific barrier against a wide range of parasites. Examples include the skin and mucous membranes that prevent microscopic parasites from entering the body. Cellular defenses, like macrophages, are also part of innate immunity and can engulf and destroy invading bacteria. In insects, this can be seen in their ability to encapsulate and kill parasitoid eggs. Innate responses are consistent and don't change with repeated exposure.

Acquired Immunity

  • Acquired Immunity: This type of immunity develops after a host has been exposed to a specific parasite. The body "remembers" the invader and can mount a stronger and faster response upon subsequent encounters. This is how vaccines work, and it's similar to the induced defenses seen in plants. In vertebrates, white blood cells, or leukocytes, play a key role by engulfing and destroying parasites. When an area is infected, inflammation often occurs, which increases blood flow and sends more immune cells to the site.

Behavioral Defenses

Animals also use specific behaviors to prevent or remove parasites.

  • Grooming: Many mammals groom themselves or others to get rid of ectoparasites like fleas and ticks.
  • Evasive Actions: Insects attacked by parasitoids may drop from a leaf to the ground to escape.
  • Social Immunity: Social insects like ants and termites, which live in close quarters, have a unique defense called social immunity. They can transfer resistance to diseases to other members of the colony. This may happen through the sharing of oral secretions from an immunized individual to an unexposed one, helping the entire colony resist infection. These insects also groom each other to remove parasites.

The Evolutionary Arms Race

An ongoing evolutionary "arms race" exists between parasites and their hosts. Parasites typically have a faster generation time, giving them a potential advantage in evolving new ways to overcome host defenses. This means hosts are constantly adapting their defenses to keep up with the rapid evolution of the parasites that target them.

Biological Control of Pests by Parasites

Biological control is the use of natural enemies, such as parasitoids and predators, to manage pests, especially those that threaten crops. This method is often seen as a beneficial alternative to chemical pesticides.

Key Attributes of a Successful Biocontrol Agent

Historically, early biological control efforts were often "hit-or-miss," involving the release of various agents in the hope that one would be effective. Later research by scientists like Carl Huffaker and Charles Kennett identified key characteristics of a good biological control agent:

Biocontrol Agent Attributes

  1. Adaptability: The agent should be able to thrive in its new environment.
  2. High Searching Capacity: It needs to be effective at finding its hosts.
  3. High Rate of Increase: The agent's population must grow quickly relative to the pest population.
  4. Good Dispersal Ability: It needs to spread effectively throughout the infested area.
  5. Minimal Time Lag: Its population should respond quickly to changes in the pest's population size.

Later, Peter Stiling's research confirmed that the most crucial factor for success is a climatic match between the agent's place of origin and the area where it's released. This is because a mismatch in climate is a leading cause of biological control failures.

Biocontrol Risks

While often promoted as risk-free, biological control can have negative consequences for non-target species. Early "hit-or-miss" campaigns sometimes led to unintended harm. For example, entomologist Frank Howarth observed that parasitoid wasps introduced to control sugarcane pests in Hawaii also attacked and reduced the populations of native Hawaiian butterflies and moths. This highlighted the need for more focused, precise release efforts to ensure that the introduced agents only target the intended pests and not native species.

Host-Parasite Models

Host-parasite models suggest many diseases need large host populations to spread. Since parasite-removal studies are difficult to perform, ecologists have also examined the strength of parasitism as a mortality factor by modeling host-parasite relationships.

Models of the effect of parasites on host populations differ from predator-prey models, for two reasons.

  1. Complex Life Cycles: Many parasites have complex life cycles involving intermediate hosts, making their transmission much more complicated to model. Modeling parasite transmission between the various types of hosts can be extremely complex, involving the densities of many hosts and parasites at several different stages.
  2. Focus on Infected Hosts: Corresponding to the diseases, models of parasite population dynamics generally describe the parasite's population growth rate by the average number of new cases of a disease that arise from each infected host, rather than by the numbers of disease-causing organisms.

Mathematical Models

We can designate the number of infected as Rp, wherein R stands for net reproductive rate and p for parasite.

  • The transmission threshold (A parasite's ability to spread), which must be exceeded if a disease is to spread, is given by Rp > 1.
  • If the value of Rp < 1, the disease perishes.

For microparasites that are transmitted directly from host to host, Rp is influenced by

  • Ns the number of susceptible hosts in the population.
  • B the transmission rate of the disease, a quantity correlated with frequency of host contact and infectiousness of the disease.
  • L is the average period of time over which the infected host remains infectious.

The Model Equation

The value Rp is related to these factors by the equation

Rp = NsBL

Three generalizations can thus be made.

  • As L, the period during which the host is infectious, upsurges, Rp also increases. An efficient parasite therefore keeps its host alive for a long time. In the extreme, some hosts remain infectious even after they are dead. For example, fungal parasites leave resting spores on the dead host.
  • If diseases are highly infectious, that is, B is large, Rp increases.
  • Large populations of susceptible hosts, Ns, promote the spread of disease.

Critical Threshold Density

By rearranging the equation the critical threshold density thus obtained is, NT, where Rp = 1. NT is an estimate of the number of susceptible hosts needed to maintain the parasite population at a constant size:

NT = 1/BL

If B or L is large, NT is small.

For example, if B = 0.1 and L = 0.01, then NT = 1/0.1 x 0.01 = 1/0.001 = 1,000.

Conversely, if B or L is small, the disease can persist only in a large population of infected hosts.

For example, if B = 0.1 and L = 0.0001, then NT = 100,000.

Disease Cycles and Control

Many diseases of humans are likely to be evolutionarily recent, having spread from other animals. Global climate change may be changing the frequency of parasitism levels of hosts and, by implication, host densities.

Similar to predator-prey dynamics, many diseases exhibit periodic cycles. For instance, measles and whooping cough in humans show predictable peaks. These cycles occur because an epidemic reduces the number of susceptible hosts (Ns​), causing the disease to decline. However, as new births add more susceptible individuals to the population, the cycle begins anew. Vaccination can effectively break these cycles by significantly reducing the number of susceptible hosts.

When parasites are spread by a vector, such as a mosquito, disease control efforts may focus on the vector rather than the host. For example, some farmers use insecticides to kill aphids that transmit viral diseases to crops.
