PREDICTING THE OUTCOMES OF GENETIC CROSSES

Section 1 of 6

One of Mendel's goals in conducting his experiments on pea plants was to develop a way to predict the outcome of crosses between plants with different phenotypes.

1. THE PUNNETT SQUARE METHOD

The Punnett square, developed by English geneticist Reginald C. Punnett in 1917, is a straightforward method for predicting the outcomes of genetic crosses. It involves drawing a grid to systematically combine the possible gametes produced by each parent to determine the resulting progeny genotypes and phenotypes.

How to Use a Punnett Square

A. Determine Parental Gametes:

The Principle of Segregation states that the two alleles for a trait in a parent separate, with one allele going into each gamete. For example, in a cross between a heterozygous tall plant (Tt) and a homozygous short plant (tt):

  • The tt parent produces only t gametes.
  • The Tt parent produces 50% T gametes and 50% t gametes.

B. Construct the Square:

  • Place the gametes from one parent along the top edge of the grid.
  • Place the gametes from the other parent down the left side.

C. Fill the Cells:

Each cell in the grid is filled by combining the corresponding alleles from the top and side, which generates the genotype of the potential offspring. It is helpful to also write the resulting phenotype (e.g., T is dominant for tallness).

D. Determine Ratios:

By counting the cells, you can determine the expected genotypic ratio and phenotypic ratio of the progeny.

Example and Applications

  • In the example cross (Tt×tt), which is a backcross (a cross between an F1​ genotype and one of the parental genotypes), the Punnett square shows:
    • Genotypes: 2 Tt to 2 tt (a 1:1 ratio).
    • Phenotypes: 1/2 tall (Tt) and 1/2 short (tt).
  • The Punnett square method is effective for situations involving one or two genes.
  • For more complex situations, like those involving more than two genes, the Punnett square becomes unwieldy, and a probability-based approach is preferred.

2. THE FORKED-LINE METHOD

The forked-line method is an alternative to the Punnett square, used primarily for predicting the outcome of crosses involving two or more independently assorting genes. Instead of using a grid to systematically list all progeny, this method uses a diagram of branching lines to tally the expected offspring.

How the Forked-Line Method Works

A. Partition the Cross:

A cross involving multiple genes (like the trihybrid intercross Dd Gg Ww×Dd Gg Ww) is broken down into separate monohybrid crosses for each gene (Dd×Dd, Gg×Gg, and Ww×Ww). This is possible because the genes assort independently.

B. Determine Ratios for Each Gene:

For an intercross between heterozygotes, each monohybrid cross is expected to yield a 3:1 phenotypic ratio (e.g., 3 tall : 1 dwarf).

C. Combine Ratios:

The forked-line diagram visually combines these individual ratios. You start with the ratio of the first gene, and then branch off from each of those outcomes with the ratio of the second gene, and so on.

D. Calculate Final Proportions:

Multiplying the proportions along each complete path of the branches gives the overall expected proportions for the combined phenotypes of the offspring (e.g., 3/4 tall ×3/4 yellow ×3/4 round leads to 27/64 tall, yellow, round progeny).

Application to a Testcross

The forked-line method can also be used for a testcross, which is a cross between a multiply heterozygous individual (Dd Gg Ww) and a multiply homozygous recessive individual (dd gg ww).

  • In this case, the homozygous recessive parent only transmits recessive alleles.
  • The outcome of the offspring's genotypes and phenotypes is determined entirely by the alleles transmitted by the heterozygous parent.
  • Because the heterozygous parent segregates the dominant and recessive alleles for each gene in a 1:1 ratio, the final phenotypic ratios of the offspring will be a combination of these 1:1 probabilities for each independently assorting gene.

3. THE PROBABILITY METHOD

Probability is a crucial tool in genetics, as utilized by Mendel, for predicting the likelihood of a specific event—in this context, the outcome of a cross. Probability is formally defined as:

Probability = Number of times a particular event occurs / Total number of all possible outcomes

For example, the probability of drawing the King of Hearts from a 52-card deck is 1/52 (1 favorable outcome out of 52 total). Probability can be expressed as a fraction (e.g., 4/52 for drawing any ace) or a decimal (e.g., 0.077).

The probability of an event can be determined in two ways:

  1. By reasoning: Understanding the nature of the event (e.g., a six-sided die has 6 equal sides, so the probability of rolling a 'four' is 1/6).
  2. Empirically (by observation): Making a large number of observations to determine the frequency of the event (e.g., a weather forecast based on past conditions).

Rules of Probability in Genetics

Two fundamental rules of probability are used to predict the ratios of offspring in genetic crosses: the multiplication rule and the addition rule.

The Multiplication Rule

The multiplication rule states that the probability of two or more independent events occurring together is calculated by multiplying their individual probabilities.

  • Formula: P(A and B)=P(A)×P(B)
  • Key Indicator: The word "and" (e.g., the probability of getting a 'four' on the first roll and a 'four' on the second roll).
  • Example: The probability of rolling a die twice and getting a 'four' both times is (1/6)×(1/6)=1/36.
  • Condition: This rule is valid only if the events are independent, meaning the outcome of one event does not influence the outcome of the other (e.g., one die roll doesn't affect the next).

The Addition Rule

The addition rule states that the probability of any one of two or more mutually exclusive events occurring is calculated by adding the probabilities of these individual events.

  • Formula: P(A or B)=P(A)+P(B)
  • Key Indicators: The words "either" and "or" (e.g., the probability of rolling either a 'three' or a 'four').
  • Example: The probability of rolling a single die and getting a 'three' or a 'four' is (1/6)+(1/6)=2/6, or 1/3.
  • Condition: This rule is valid only if the events are mutually exclusive, meaning the occurrence of one event excludes the possibility of the other event occurring (e.g., you cannot roll both a 'three' and a 'four' on a single toss of one die).

Using Probability to Solve Genetic Crosses

The multiplication rule and addition rule of probability offer a powerful alternative to the Punnett square for predicting the ratios of offspring from a genetic cross. While the Punnett square is simpler for single-gene crosses (monohybrid crosses), the probability method is quicker and clearer for more complex crosses involving multiple genes.

Applying the Rules to a Monohybrid Cross (Tt×Tt)

  1. Determine Gamete Probability: Since each heterozygous parent (Tt) produces T and t gametes in equal proportions, the probability for each gamete type is 1/2.
  2. Use the Multiplication Rule for Genotypes: To find the probability of a specific offspring genotype, the multiplication rule is used because the allele inherited from one parent and the allele inherited from the second parent are independent events.
    • TT: P(T from P1)×P(T from P2)=(1/2)×(1/2)=1/4
    • tt: P(t from P1)×P(t from P2)=(1/2)×(1/2)=1/4
    • Tt (two ways): P(T from P1 and t from P2)=1/4 or P(t from P1 and T from P2)=1/4
  3. Use the Addition Rule for Phenotypes: To find the probability of an overall phenotype, the addition rule is used because a tall plant can be TT, Tt, or tT (mutually exclusive events).
    • P(Tall)=P(TT)+P(Tt)+P(tT)=1/4+1/4+1/4=3/4
    • P(Short)=P(tt)=1/4

The Binomial Expansion for Predicting Family Outcomes

When a cross can result in several ways for a set of events to occur (like predicting the gender and traits of multiple children), the binomial expansion simplifies the calculations.

Example: Albinism in Three Children

For parents heterozygous for albinism (Aa×Aa), the probability of an affected child (aa) is p=1/4, and the probability of an unaffected child is q=3/4.

  • Simple Case (Only One Way): The probability of having three children, all with albinism, is a direct application of the multiplication rule: P(aaa)=(1/4)×(1/4)×(1/4)=1/64.
  • Complex Case (Multiple Ways): The probability of having one child with albinism and two unaffected requires the addition rule because there are three possible sequences (e.g., affected-unaffected-unaffected, unaffected-affected-unaffected, or unaffected-unaffected-affected).
    • Probability of one specific sequence: (1/4)×(3/4)×(3/4)=9/64.
    • Overall Probability: 9/64+9/64+9/64=27/64.

Comparing Observed and Expected Genetic Ratios

In genetic crosses, expected ratios of genotypes and phenotypes are based on Mendelian principles like segregation and dominance (e.g., a 1:1 or 3:1 ratio). However, due to chance (similar to coin flipping), the observed numbers in the progeny often deviate from the expected perfect ratios.

  • Example: A cross between a heterozygous brown cockroach (Yy) and a yellow cockroach (yy) is expected to produce a 1:1 ratio (20 brown : 20 yellow) out of 40 offspring. Observing 22 brown and 18 yellow is a small, reasonable deviation likely due to chance.

However, a large deviation (e.g., 25 brown and 15 yellow) might indicate that something other than chance is at play, such as a more complex inheritance pattern or reduced survival of one genotype (e.g., death of some yellow progeny).

The Goodness-of-Fit Chi-Square Test (χ2)

To objectively evaluate whether the difference between observed and expected results is likely due to chance, scientists use a statistical tool called the goodness-of-fit chi-square test (χ2).

What the χ2 Test Does and Doesn't Do

  • What it does: It provides the probability that the deviation between the observed and expected values is due to chance alone. This probability evaluates the null hypothesis, which states that chance is responsible for any observed differences.
  • What it doesn't do: It cannot prove that a cross was performed correctly, that the results are right, or that the genetic model chosen is correct.

Example Calculation (Cats)

If two heterozygous black cats (Bb×Bb) are crossed, a 3:1 ratio is expected. Out of 50 kittens:

  1. Observed Values: 30 black, 20 gray.
  2. Expected Values:
    • Black: (3/4)×50=37.5
    • Gray: (1/4)×50=12.5
  3. Calculate χ2:
    • Black contribution: (30−37.5)2/37.5 = (−7.5)2/37.5=56.25/37.5=1.5
    • Gray contribution: (20−12.5)2/12.5 = (7.5)2/12.5=56.25/12.5=4.5
    • Total χ2 Value: 1.5+4.5=6.0

Interpreting the χ2 Probability

  1. Determine Degrees of Freedom (df): For a goodness-of-fit test, df equals the number of expected phenotypic classes (n) minus one: df=n−1.
    • In the cat example, there are two phenotypes (black and gray), so df=2−1=1.
  2. Find the Probability: The calculated χ2 value (6.0) is compared to a χ2 table for the corresponding df.
    • For df=1, a χ2 value of 6.0 falls between the theoretical values associated with a probability of 0.025 and 0.01. This means the probability of getting this large of a deviation by chance alone is less than 2.5% and greater than 1%.

Significance and the Cutoff Value

  • Cutoff Value: Most scientists use a 0.05 probability level (or 5%) as the standard cutoff.
  • High Probability (≥0.05): If the calculated probability is ≥0.05, the null hypothesis is accepted; the deviation is considered likely due to chance.
  • Low Probability (<0.05): If the calculated probability is <0.05 (like the 0.01−0.025 range for the cats), a significant difference exists. This means the null hypothesis is rejected, and some factor other than chance (like reduced viability or a different genetic mechanism) must be responsible for the observed results.

Note: Even with a very low probability (e.g., 0.01), there is still a small chance (1%) that the deviation was indeed due to random chance.
