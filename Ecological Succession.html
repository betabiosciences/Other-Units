ECOLOGICAL SUCCESSION

Ecological succession is the ongoing, directional shift in species composition and community structure that occurs in an area over time. This continuous change is triggered by a disturbance, which can range from large-scale natural events like volcanic eruptions, earthquakes, hurricanes, and fires, to localized human impacts such as land-clearing and other activities. Succession is thus the process by which an ecosystem gradually recovers and reorganizes itself following an interruption.

Key Aspects of Ecological Succession

Succession is a fundamental concept in ecology, described by the following processes:

  • Directional Change: The sequence of species and community types is not random. It generally proceeds through a predictable series of stages, from early colonizers (pioneer species) to a stable, mature, and self-sustaining climax community.
  • Species Replacement: In each successional stage (or sere), one set of species is replaced by another. The initial colonizers modify the environment (e.g., changing soil chemistry, increasing shade), making it suitable for the next set of species but often less suitable for themselves.
  • Scale of Disturbance: The process begins following a disturbance that removes some or all of the existing biomass. Disturbances can be abiotic (e.g., drought, flood) or biotic (e.g., disease outbreak, insect infestation).
  • Time: Succession is a process that occurs over long periods, from decades (in the case of abandoned farmland) to centuries or millennia (in the case of primary succession on bare rock).

Types of Succession

1. Primary and Secondary Succession

Ecologists classify community recovery into two main types based on the initial conditions: primary succession and secondary succession.

Primary Succession

Primary succession occurs on a site that has been newly exposed and was previously unoccupied by both soil and vegetation. This process is triggered by extreme events that create completely barren ground, such as:

  • Bare rock exposed by a retreating glacier.
  • New land formed by volcanic eruptions (e.g., Mount St. Helens, Hawaii, or Iceland).

Because the initial surface lacks soil, the colonizing plants must first help build up the substrate, a process that can take a very long time—potentially hundreds of years. Primary succession is currently occurring over only a tiny fraction of the Earth's surface.

Figure 2: Primary succession on bare rock

Secondary Succession

Secondary succession occurs on a site that has already supported life and where the soil remains intact after a disturbance. Since the soil, along with many microorganisms (like bacteria, nematodes, and insects) and plant seeds, is still present, the recovery process is much faster. Disturbances that lead to secondary succession include:

  • Natural disasters like fire, tornado, hurricane, or flood.
  • Human activities like clearing a forest for farming.

A classic example is the recovery of abandoned farmlands, often called old fields. In this scenario, the history of farming—including plowing and the addition of fertilizers, herbicides, and pesticides—can fundamentally alter the soil's chemistry. This altered soil may favor the colonization of species (e.g., those requiring high nitrogen levels) that would not be present in the early stages of primary succession on nutrient-poor volcanic or glacial soils. The resulting pattern of vegetation is therefore often quite distinct from that of a primary succession site.

2. Autogenic and Allogenic Succession

Autogenic and allogenic succession describe two different drivers behind the ecological process of succession within communities.

Autogenic Succession

Autogenic succession refers to changes in an ecological community driven primarily by internal modifications caused by organisms already present in that community. Species influence their own environment through biological activities, which can alter physical and chemical conditions over time. These changes might include:

  • Alteration of nutrient availability through photosynthesis, decomposition, or nitrogen fixation.
  • Modification of the light environment, such as shading by the growing canopy.
  • Changes in soil structure or composition due to root growth or litter accumulation.

As a consequence, the community becomes less favorable for the current inhabitants and more suitable for new colonizers adapted to the modified conditions. This self-driven process shapes the course and direction of succession in a predictable manner within the same habitat, given similar starting conditions.

Allogenic (Heterogenic) Succession

Allogenic succession, by contrast, is initiated by external, non-biological factors rather than the biotic community itself. These factors include changes brought about by environmental influences such as:

  • Flooding, fires, storms, or other natural disasters.
  • Changes in climate or weather patterns.
  • Human activities like land clearance, logging, or urbanization.

Allogenic succession results in the replacement of an existing community because of these outside disturbances, regardless of the biological modifications made by the inhabitants. Unlike autogenic succession, allogenic forces may abruptly alter the ecosystem, resulting in new conditions and sometimes triggering a faster or more unpredictable succession trajectory.

In summary, autogenic succession is driven by the inhabitants' self-modification of their environment, while allogenic succession is triggered by abiotic, external factors causing community replacement. Both processes are fundamental in understanding patterns and pathways of ecological succession within natural systems.

3. Autotrophic and Heterotrophic Succession

Autotrophic and heterotrophic succession refer to two distinct types of ecological succession based on the primary mode of energy acquisition and dominant forms of organic matter production within the community.

Autotrophic Succession

Autotrophic succession is characterized by the predominance of organisms called autotrophs, which are capable of synthesizing their own food from inorganic substances. Most notably, these are green plants and certain algae that fix carbon through photosynthesis.

  • The majority of the living biomass in autotrophic succession is plant matter.
  • Organic matter input into the ecosystem is largely fixed by these photosynthetic organisms, which form the base of the food web.
  • Such successions are typical in environments where inorganic resources like light, water, and simple nutrients are abundant, such as in newly exposed land or aquatic substrates after disturbances.
  • Over time, these autotrophs modify the environment, increasing organic content and paving the way for more complex community development.

Heterotrophic (Degradative) Succession

Heterotrophic, or degradative, succession is centered around the breakdown and utilization of pre-existing organic materials by heterotrophs—organisms that rely on consuming complex organic substances for energy.

  • Most of the living biomass in heterotrophic succession consists of animals, fungi, and various microbial organisms.
  • Succession in this case starts from a substrate rich in degradable organic matter, such as dead plant material, animal remains, or microbial mats.
  • The initial phases are dominated by decomposers and detritivores, which exploit the available organic resources.
  • As these resources are exhausted, the community structure changes, often giving way eventually to autotrophic organisms if environmental conditions allow.

In summary, autotrophic succession is dominated by plants and other autotrophs fixing new organic matter, whereas heterotrophic succession is propelled by the breakdown of existing organic matter mainly by animals, fungi, and microbes. These two pathways represent fundamental differences in the driving forces and energy flow during ecological succession.

4. Types of Succession Based on Substratum

Succession can be classified according to the type of substratum (surface or environment) on which it begins. The substratum influences the conditions and development trajectory of the successional community. There are three main types:

Hydroseral or Hydrarch Succession

  • This type of succession begins in freshwater environments.
  • Examples include ponds, lakes, streams, and other freshwater bodies.
  • The successional sequence here often involves aquatic plants, algae, and eventually the development of terrestrial vegetation as the water body fills with sediment and organic matter over time.

Xerosere or Xerarch Succession

Succession beginning in dry or arid conditions.

This type is further divided into three subtypes based on the exact nature of the dry substratum:

  • Lithosere: Succession that starts on bare rocks. This includes pioneering lichens and mosses that can colonize rock surfaces and initiate soil formation.
  • Psammosere: Succession that begins on sandy substrates, such as dunes or sand plains. Plants that can tolerate sandy, nutrient-poor conditions initially dominate.
  • Halosere: Succession that starts in saline conditions like salt marshes or coastal saline soils. Salt-tolerant species are the early colonizers.

Mesarch Succession

  • Succession that begins on mesic, or moderately moist and well-balanced, environmental conditions.
  • Mesic conditions are intermediate between aquatic and dry environments and tend to support a wide range of plant and animal species during succession.

This classification highlights the importance of the initial environment in determining the community structure and successional pathway, as the physical and chemical properties of the substratum directly affect which organisms can establish and develop.

Frederic Clements's Succession Theory: Climax and Mechanisms

Frederic Clements, widely regarded as the founder of succession theory in the early 20th century, proposed a framework where community change is predictable and culminates in a stable, distinct end point called the climax community.

Key Concepts of Clements's Theory

Clements viewed succession as a highly directional process characterized by the following concepts:

  • Sere and Seral Stage: Each distinct phase of community change along the path to climax was termed a sere or seral stage. The very first stage of colonization was known as the pioneer seral stage.
  • Directional Change: The community typically progresses toward the climax. However, a disturbance could push a community from a later stage back to an earlier one.
  • Cyclic Succession: Clements suggested that in the absence of disturbance, a community would eventually reach the climax stage and then enter a state of continuous, non-progressive replacement of the same seral stage, known as cyclic succession. Conversely, he also noted that frequent disturbances could prevent the community from ever achieving a true climax.
  • Mechanism of Facilitation: The core mechanism driving succession, according to Clements, was facilitation. This key assumption held that each colonizing species alters the environment—for instance, by enriching soil nitrogen—making it more suitable for the invasion of subsequent, competing species. This process continues until the competitively dominant species, characteristic of the climax community, are established.
  • Climax Determination: The final composition of the climax community in any given region was thought to be primarily determined by regional climate and soil conditions.

Subsequent Models

While Clements emphasized the mechanism of facilitation, later research by Joe Connell and Ralph Slatyer introduced two other mechanisms that also influence successional pathways: inhibition and tolerance.

Connell and Slatyer's Three Models of Succession

While Frederic Clements focused exclusively on facilitation, the later models proposed by Joe Connell and Ralph Slatyer in 1977 provided a broader, more empirically supported framework by classifying successional processes into three distinct mechanisms based on how early-stage species influence the establishment of later-stage species.

Model Mechanism Description of Early Species' Effect on Late Species Outcome
1. Facilitation (Clements's Model) Positive Interaction Early species modify the environment, making it more suitable for the next wave of colonists. Succession proceeds as a predictable, directional relay, often leading to climax.
2. Tolerance Neutral Interaction Early species have no effect (neither helpful nor harmful) on the establishment of late species. Late species establish successfully simply because they can tolerate the low resource levels/harsh conditions better than early species, and are typically better competitors. Sequence is determined primarily by the life history (e.g., longevity, dispersal) and competitive ability of the late species.
3. Inhibition Negative Interaction Early species modify the environment, making it less suitable (e.g., through competition, allelopathy, predation) for the invasion and growth of later species. Succession only progresses when early species are removed by stress or disturbance, or when they naturally die off.

Key Critique of Clements's Climax Concept

The major challenge to Clements's theory stemmed from his view of the climax community as a highly integrated, single endpoint for a given region—often called the superorganism concept.

  • Monoclimax vs. Polyclimax: Clements proposed the monoclimax hypothesis, suggesting that climate alone dictates the final stable community, which is universal across a large geographic area. However, later ecologists, notably Henry Gleason and A. G. Tansley, argued for a polyclimax view, recognizing that local factors (such as soil, topography, water table, and frequent disturbance) create multiple stable endpoints (edaphic, topographic, or fire climaxes) within the same climatic region.
  • The Individualistic Concept (Gleason): In direct opposition to Clements's integrated "superorganism" view, Gleason proposed the individualistic concept of the plant association. This theory argues that communities are simply collections of species whose presence in a given place is determined by the independent response of each species to the abiotic environment (temperature, moisture, soil) and its own unique life history characteristics. Therefore, community boundaries are gradual, and the concept of a single, highly integrated climax community is an arbitrary construct.
  • Modern Consensus: Modern ecology generally supports a modified individualistic view. While succession is often directional, it is rarely as tightly controlled by facilitation as Clements described. Real-world succession is often a mix of all three mechanisms (facilitation, tolerance, and inhibition), and the final community structure is highly variable and context-dependent.

Mechanisms of Succession

1. Facilitation

Facilitation is a key ecological mechanism in succession, particularly prevalent in harsh or resource-limited environments. It is defined by the process where early-colonizing species modify the environment in a way that makes it more suitable for later-arriving species, thus driving the directional change toward a climax community.

Key Principles of the Facilitation Model

  1. Positive Species Interaction: Facilitation represents a positive, non-trophic interaction where the presence of one species directly or indirectly benefits the establishment and survival of another species.
  2. Environmental Modification: Pioneer species create beneficial changes, often ameliorating harsh abiotic conditions. Common modifications include:
    • Nutrient Enrichment (e.g., Nitrogen Fixation): As seen in Glacier Bay, nitrogen-fixing bacteria associated with plants like cyanobacteria and Dryas and Alnus enrich nutrient-poor soil, making it suitable for larger, nitrogen-demanding trees like spruce.
    • Soil and Substrate Stabilization: Plants like beach grass stabilize shifting substrates (e.g., sand dunes, rocky coastlines), reducing erosion and providing a stable platform for later-stage, rooted vegetation.
    • Organic Matter Accumulation: Pioneers contribute litterfall, increasing soil depth, moisture retention, and organic content.
    • Roughness and Habitat Creation: In aquatic systems, early colonizers can create a rougher surface (e.g., hydroids) that enhances the settlement and attachment for larvae of later species.
  3. Self-Limitation (Replacement): The environment modified by the pioneer species is often less suitable for the pioneers themselves. For example, shade created by nitrogen-fixing alders ultimately leads to their own competitive exclusion by the shade-tolerant spruce. This ensures the continuous progression of the seral stages.
  4. Dominance in Primary Succession: Facilitation is often the dominant mechanism in the early stages of primary succession (where initial conditions are extreme, such as bare rock or lava) because the environment must first be made habitable before complex life forms can establish.
  5. Contrast with Later Stages: While crucial initially, the importance of facilitation often wanes in later successional stages, where mechanisms like tolerance (for light and competition) and inhibition (through intense competition or shading) become more influential in determining the final community structure.

The long-term glacial retreat at Glacier Bay, Alaska, provides a classic and well-documented example of this process.

The Glacier Bay Chronosequence

The succession at Glacier Bay offers a unique chronosequence—a series of communities across a physical distance primarily influenced by the time elapsed since the glacial retreat. Historical records, dating back to Captain George Vancouver's visit in 1794, and permanent study plots established by William Cooper in 1916, allow scientists to trace the distinct changes in vegetation over time.

The Successional Pattern (Seral Stages):

  1. Pioneer Stage (Initial Years): Glaciers leave behind moraines (deposits of debris) that form the parent material for soil. The initial bare ground is characterized by low nitrogen content and scant organic matter.
    • Pioneer Species: A black crust of cyanobacteria (nitrogen fixers), mosses, lichens, horsetails, and river beauty (Chamerion latifolium).
    • Facilitation: The cyanobacteria slightly increase soil nitrogen, but soil depth and litterfall remain minimal.
  2. Intermediate Stage (Around 40–60 Years):
    • Dominant Species: Dryas drummondii (a dwarf shrub) dominates. This plant contains Frankia bacteria which are potent nitrogen fixers.
    • Facilitation: Soil nitrogen, depth, and litterfall all increase significantly. This enrichment enables the invasion of alders (Alnus sinuata).
  3. Alder Thicket Stage (Around 60 Years):
    • Dominant Species: Alders form dense thickets. Alders also house nitrogen-fixing Frankia bacteria in their roots, leading to a dramatic increase in soil nitrogen and litterfall.
    • Facilitation: The nitrogen-rich soil is now suitable for the invasion of spruce trees (Picea sitchensis).
  4. Climax Stage (75 to 200+ Years):
    • Species Replacement: Spruce trees grow tall and begin to overtop the alders, shading them out (an example of competition/inhibition replacing facilitation).
    • Further Facilitation/Modification: Spruce needles create a large volume of acidic litterfall. The intense shade leads to the competitive exclusion of many original understory species, leaving only mosses.
    • Climax: After 200 years, a stable mixed spruce-hemlock forest (including Tsuga species) results.

While facilitation (specifically increased soil nitrogen and depth) fuels the early stages of succession, competition plays a large role as later invaders ultimately outcompete and exclude the earlier colonists.

Other Evidence of Facilitation

The facilitation model is supported by evidence in diverse ecological communities:

1. Dune and Salt Marsh Stabilization:

  • Sand Dunes (Lake Michigan, studied by Cowles): Salt-tolerant plants like beach grass (Ammophila breviligulata) with extensive root systems stabilize the shifting sand, making the environment suitable for later, larger species like dune willow, cottonwoods, and eventually, trees like basswood.
  • Salt Marshes (New England): Spartina grass facilitates the establishment of beach plant communities by stabilizing the rocky substrate and reducing water velocity, which allows other seedlings to successfully emerge.

2. Aquatic Communities (Settlement Sites):

  • Facilitation also occurs in marine environments where one species enhances the quality of settlement sites for others.
  • Delaware Bay Experiment: Researchers found that hydroids facilitated the settlement of tunicates, and both then facilitated the settlement of mussels (the dominant climax species). The initial rough surface created by the hydroids made the substrate more suitable for subsequent species, as the original smooth test plates had prevented most colonization.

2. Inhibition

Inhibition is a model of succession where early-colonizing species actively prevent or delay the establishment and growth of later species, primarily by monopolizing critical resources or space. In this view, possession of space is paramount, and the species that establishes first largely determines the subsequent community structure by excluding later potential colonists.

Key Principles of the Inhibition Model

  1. Negative Species Interaction: Inhibition involves a negative interaction where the presence of an early species is detrimental to a later species. This is often an extreme form of competition or allelopathy (releasing toxic chemicals).
  2. Priority Effect (First Come, First Served): The core of the inhibition model is the "priority effect," where the species that arrives first and successfully establishes possession of the site (e.g., covering the substrate or monopolizing nutrients) dominates the community structure for a potentially long time.
  3. Mechanism of Exclusion: Inhibition works through several methods:
    • Resource Depletion/Competition: Early species use up the limiting resources (like light, water, or nutrients) so extensively that later species cannot establish.
    • Toxicity (Allelopathy): Species release chemical compounds that are toxic to potential invaders (e.g., the release of toxic compounds from decomposing Setaria litter).
    • Physical Obstruction: Dense mats or thickets (like bamboo or thick grass) physically block the germination or growth of subsequent species.
    • Chemical Alteration: Early species can drastically change soil chemistry to their advantage (e.g., Tamarix increasing soil salinity).
  4. Succession Depends on Disturbance: Unlike the facilitation model, which progresses through positive change, succession under the inhibition model is essentially stalled until the early, inhibiting species are removed. Community turnover is driven by the differential mortality of the early colonists, meaning they must be killed by:
    • Physical Stress (e.g., harsh weather, waves).
    • Disturbance (e.g., fire, storm).
    • Herbivory/Predation (e.g., crabs grazing on Ulva).
  5. Relevance to High-Stress/Limited-Space Environments: Inhibition is particularly important in environments where space is the overwhelming limiting factor (like the rocky marine intertidal zone) or in nutrient-rich but disturbed sites like old fields, where fast-growing pioneer species can quickly dominate and monopolize resources.

Mechanisms and Examples of Inhibition

Inhibition occurs when early species negatively impact the establishment or growth of others through various mechanisms:

  • Chemical/Litter-Based Inhibition:
    • In New Jersey old fields, the early successional grass, giant foxtail (Setaria faberi), inhibits the growth of the later species, eastern daisy fleabane (Erigeron annuus). Removing the Setaria litter allows the Erigeron biomass to increase and dominate, suggesting that either toxic compounds released from the decomposing litter or the physical obstruction of the litter itself causes the inhibition.
    • Invasive species frequently employ inhibition: Invasive Tamarix trees in the Southwestern U.S. and Australia increase soil salts, and invasive Pinus trees in South Africa produce nutrient-poor litter that lowers soil nutrient availability, both of which inhibit normal succession.
  • Physical Obstruction and Competition: Plant species that form dense thickets—such as certain grasses, ferns, vines, pine trees, and bamboo—can physically inhibit the establishment of diverse later species.

Inhibition in the Marine Intertidal Zone

Inhibition is often considered the primary method of succession in the marine intertidal zone, where space is a severely limited resource.

  • Sousa's Experiment (1979): Wayne Sousa tested succession by clearing rock faces or placing new blocks. The areas were initially colonized by green algae (Ulva spp.). Much later, these were naturally replaced by the larger red algae (Chondracanthus canaliculatus).
  • Results: When Sousa experimentally removed the Ulva from the substrate, Chondracanthus was able to colonize much more quickly. This demonstrated that the early colonist (Ulva) was inhibiting the later species' invasion.

How Succession Occurs Under Inhibition

If early species inhibit later ones, succession only occurs when the early colonists are eliminated. In the intertidal zone, this happens because early colonizers like Ulva are more susceptible to environmental stress and to attacks by herbivores (such as Pachygraspus crabs) than the more robust, later successional species like Chondracanthus. Thus, the turnover is driven by the differential mortality of the early, inhibiting species.

3. Tolerance

The tolerance model, proposed by Joseph Connell and Ralph Slatyer (1977), represents the middle ground between the extremes of facilitation and inhibition. This model suggests that the early colonists neither facilitate nor inhibit subsequent colonists.

Key Principles of the Tolerance Model

  1. Neutral Interaction: The early successional species have a neutral effect on later species. Their presence doesn't make the habitat better or worse for new arrivals.
  2. Any Species Can Initiate: Succession can be started by any species that happens to colonize the disturbed area first. This initial composition is largely determined by chance factors, such as which seeds or roots are present in the soil.
  3. Orderly Climax via Competition: Although the initial colonization is random, the eventual climax community is still reached in a somewhat orderly fashion. This progression occurs because larger, slower-growing, and long-lived species possess superior competitive ability. They eventually invade and outcompete the smaller, fast-growing pioneer species, leading to species turnover and the eventual dominance of the climax species (e.g., forest trees).
  4. Evidence (Egler's Work): Connell and Slatyer drew support from Frank Egler's (1954) earlier work on secondary succession in Connecticut. Egler showed that succession was primarily determined by species already present in the ground as buried seeds or old roots. A random disturbance, like a light gap created by a treefall, allows whichever species is closest to germinate first, initiating the sequence. However, the superior competitors ultimately prevail.

Distinction from Other Models

The core distinction among the three models lies in how earlier species influence later species:

  • Facilitation: Previous colonists help (facilitate) later colonists.
  • Inhibition: Previous colonists hurt (inhibit) later colonists.
  • Tolerance: Previous colonists do not affect (are tolerated by) later colonists. The sequence is driven by the late species' ability to tolerate the environment and outcompete the early species.

General Process of Succession

Succession is completed in five steps namely nudation, invasion, competition and coaction, reaction, and stabilization.

  1. Nudation - this is the development of a vacant area without any form of life. This area may develop due to number of causes like landslides, erosion, deposition or other catastrophic agency. There are multiple causes of nudation including
    • Topographic -- Due to soil erosion by gravity or wind, the prevailing community may disappear. Other causes may be deposition of sand, landslide, volcanic activity and other factors.
    • Climatic - Glaciers, dry period, hails and storm, frost, fire etc may be responsible for destroying the community.
    • Biotic - man is most important factor, responsible for destruction of forests and grasslands for industry, agriculture, housing etc. besides some other factors are disease epidemic due to fungi, viruses etc. which may destroy the whole population.
  2. Invasion - this is the stage of successful establishment of a species in a bare area. The species infact reaches this new site from some other area. This whole process is completed in following three successive stages.
    • Migration (dispersal) - the seeds, spores or other propagules of the species reach the bare area through migration, which is generally brought about by air water etc.
    • Ecesis (Establishment) - After reaching to new area, the process of successful establishment of the species by adapting to the conditions prevailing there, is known as ecesis. In plants, after migration, seeds or propagules germinate, seedlings grow and adult start reproducing. However only a few of them are capable of doing this under primitive harsh conditions and the rest of them disappear. Thus, as a result of ecesis, the individuals of species become established in the area.
    • Aggregation - After ecesis, as a result of reproduction, the individuals of the species increase in number and they come close to each other. This process is known as aggregation.
  3. Competition and Coaction - After aggregation of a large number of individuals of the species at the limited place, there develops competition (inter as well as intraspecific) mainly for space and nutrition. Individuals of a species influence each others life in various ways and this is called coaction. The species, if unable to compete with other species, if present, would be discarded. To survive competition, reprocductive capacity, wide ecological amplitude etc are of much help to the species.
  4. Reaction - This is the most crucial stage in succession. The mechanism of the alteration of the environment through the influence of living organisms on it is known as reaction. As a result of reactions, changes take place in soil, water, light conditions, temperature etc. of the environment. Due to all these the environment is modified, becoming unsuitable for the existing community which sooner or later is replaced by another community (seral stage). The whole process of the replacement of one community by another is called reaction.
  5. Stabilization (Climax) - This is the final stage of succession. The succession ultimately results in a stabilized community which is in equilibrium with the environment. This final community is called the climax community and the stage is called climax stage.

Changes Involved in Succession

During succession species that colonize in early seral stages tend to have different life-history features than those that colonize in later seral stages.

Early seral stage species are often wind-dispersed, and their seeds can live a long time, thus maximizing their chances of successful colonization. In addition, plants in the early stages of succession acquire nutrients quickly, grow faster, and reach maturity at low biomass.

Species in the latter stages of succession are larger and more successful competitors, who grow slowly and whose seeds are dispersed by animals. They can grow in low light levels characteristic of closed forests. Species in later seral stage communities are relatively resistant to disturbance.

Key Terms and Definitions

Term Definition
Succession The directional change in species composition and community structure over time following a disturbance.
Primary Succession Succession that begins on a site without soil, such as bare rock or volcanic ash.
Secondary Succession Succession that begins on a site where soil is already present, following a disturbance that removed vegetation.
Climax Community The relatively stable, mature community that results from successional processes, characterized by species that can replace themselves.
Sere The entire sequence of communities that replaces one another in a given area during succession.
Pioneer Species The first species to colonize a disturbed area, typically with high dispersal ability and rapid growth.
Facilitation A mechanism of succession where early species modify the environment to make it more suitable for later species.
Tolerance A mechanism of succession where later species can establish in the presence of early species because they are better competitors.
Inhibition A mechanism of succession where early species prevent the establishment of later species, and succession only proceeds when early species are removed.
Chronosequence A series of sites of different ages that represent different stages of succession, used to study successional processes.
