PREDATION

Introduction to Predation

Predation, an ecological interaction where one organism, the predator, kills and consumes another organism, the prey, is a fundamental force shaping ecosystems worldwide. This dynamic relationship is not merely a matter of life and death; it is a critical driver of natural selection, influencing the evolution of both predator and prey species. Predators have evolved an astonishing array of hunting strategies, from the stealthy ambush of a spider to the coordinated pack hunting of wolves, while prey species have developed equally diverse defense mechanisms, including camouflage, mimicry, and the ability to outrun their pursuers.

Consider the classic example of the cheetah and the gazelle on the African savanna. The cheetah, a predator, has evolved a body built for incredible speed, with a flexible spine and long legs that allow it to reach bursts of over 100 km/h. This adaptation is a direct result of the selective pressure exerted by its prey---the gazelle. The gazelle, in turn, has evolved its own counter-adaptations: an acute sense of hearing, powerful legs for a quick start, and an erratic, zig-zagging running pattern that makes it difficult for the cheetah to predict its movements. This ongoing "co-evolutionary arms race" pushes both species to become faster and more agile over generations.

Examples of Predation Strategies

Orchid Mantis - Ambush Predation

Another fascinating example is the relationship between the orchid mantis and its insect prey. The orchid mantis has evolved a body that perfectly mimics the petals of an orchid flower. It sits motionless, blending seamlessly into its floral surroundings, and waits for an unsuspecting bee or butterfly to land nearby. This ambush strategy is a highly specialized form of predation that relies on deception rather than speed. In response, many insects have evolved keen eyesight and a wary approach to flowers, learning to distinguish between a harmless bloom and a hidden predator.

Killer Whales - Cooperative Hunting

On the other end of the spectrum is the group hunting of killer whales (orcas). These marine predators work together in sophisticated pods to corner and capture seals, dolphins, and even larger whales. They use complex communication and coordinated strategies, such as creating a wave to wash a seal off an ice floe. This cooperative hunting is a powerful example of a social adaptation for predation. The prey, in this case, has also evolved defenses, such as living in large, well-defended herds or using their own social structures to create a protective barrier around their young.

These examples highlight how predation is a powerful evolutionary force. Predators that are more effective at hunting are more likely to survive and reproduce, passing on their traits to the next generation. Simultaneously, prey that are more effective at escaping are also more likely to pass on their genes. This continuous process of natural selection shapes the physical, behavioural, and even social characteristics of species, creating the incredible diversity of life we see in ecosystems today.

Anti-predator Adaptations

Animals have developed a wide range of adaptations to avoid being eaten. These antipredator adaptations can be categorized into several main types, including chemical defenses, camouflage, mimicry, physical defenses, intimidation, and predator satiation.

Chemical Defenses and Warning Coloration

Many animals use chemicals to deter predators. The bombardier beetle is a prime example, using a hot, violent spray of chemicals from its abdomen to fend off attackers. Similarly, some millipedes also employ chemical sprays. Often, animals with chemical defenses also have aposematic coloration, or warning coloration, to advertise their toxicity. Ladybird beetles use their bright red color, while many tropical frogs have vibrant patterns that warn of their lethal skin toxins. Some animals, like the monarch butterfly, acquire their toxins by eating specific plants, in this case, milkweed, which contains cardiac glycosides that cause predators to vomit. The butterflies then display their toxicity with their bold orange and black wings.

Cryptic Coloration (Camouflage)

Cryptic coloration is a form of camouflage where an animal blends in with its environment to avoid detection. This is a common strategy for both invertebrates and vertebrates. For instance, some grasshoppers are green to match the foliage they eat, while leaf-wing butterflies have wings that perfectly mimic leaves, including the veins. Stick insects have long, slender bodies that resemble twigs. Many of these animals remain perfectly still when threatened to enhance their camouflage. Seahorses also use cryptic coloration, with their body shape and color patterns mimicking their specific habitats.

Mimicry

Mimicry involves one species (the mimic) resembling another species (the model) for protection. There are two main types:

Batesian Mimicry

  • Batesian Mimicry, named after the English naturalist Henry Bates. A harmless, palatable species(the mimic) mimics a dangerous, unpalatable one(the model). The non-venomous scarlet king snake mimics the venomous coral snake to deter predators. Similarly, some syrphid flies have black and yellow stripes to resemble stinging bees and wasps, even though they are harmless.

Müllerian Mimicry

  • Müllerian Mimicry, named after the German biologist Fritz Muller. Multiple unpalatable or noxious species evolve to look similar, reinforcing the warning signal. Many toxic species mimics each other and look the same, thus reinforcing the basic distasteful design. This is seen in the black and yellow stripes of many different species of bees and wasps. The monarch, queen, and viceroy butterflies also exhibit this, as all three are distasteful to predators and share similar wing patterns. Mullerian mimicry is also found among toxic Amazonian butterflies.

Physical Defenses and Intimidation

Physical Defenses and Intimidation Displays

Animals also use physical features to protect themselves. The tough shells of tortoises and turtles provide a strong defense. Many beetles have a hard exoskeleton. Animals like gazelles and flying fish use their agility and speed to escape predators. Some animals use horns and antlers for defense. Invertebrates like scorpions and crabs have claws or venomous stingers.

Some animals use intimidation displays to scare off potential predators. A toad will inflate itself with air to appear larger, while a frilled lizard will extend its neck collar to create a similar effect. A porcupine fish also inflates its body when threatened, making it seem much larger and more difficult to eat.

Predator Satiation

Predator satiation is a strategy where a large number of offspring are produced at the same time to overwhelm predators, ensuring some will survive. The most famous example is the 13- and 17-year periodical cicadas. By emerging in massive, synchronized swarms every 13 or 17 years (both prime numbers), they satiate any predators in the area, allowing a significant portion of the population to survive. The use of prime numbers for their emergence cycles ensures that predators with shorter life cycles cannot rely on them as a consistent food source.

Predator Adaptations

Predators, in turn, have evolved their own strategies to catch prey. Aggressive mimicry is one such adaptation, where a predator mimics a harmless model to get close to its prey. Crab spiders and praying mantises mimic flowers or leaves to ambush insects. The humpback anglerfish uses a bioluminescent lure to attract prey, and the alligator snapping turtle wiggles its worm-like tongue to attract fish. Other examples include female bolas spiders that mimic female moth pheromones to attract males, and the saber-toothed blenny that mimics a cleaner wrasse to get close enough to bite off a piece of a fish's fin. The zone-tailed hawk even mimics a turkey vulture's flight to get close to its prey before ambushing it.

Lotka-Volterra Equations for Predator-prey Interactions

The predator-prey relationship can be understood using the Lotka-Volterra models, which are based on population growth principles.

Prey Population Growth

The model starts with the basic assumption that a prey population grows exponentially in the absence of predators. The rate of this growth is represented by the formula:

dN/dt​=rN

Here, N is the number of prey and r is their intrinsic growth rate. When predators are introduced, they consume prey. The rate of prey consumption depends on the predator's searching efficiency (s), the number of predators (P), and the number of prey (N). This consumption is represented by the term sPN. Subtracting this from the prey's growth rate gives us the full equation for the prey population:

dN/dt ​=rN−sPN

Predator Population Growth

The predator population's birth rate depends on how many prey they consume and how efficiently they can convert that food into new predators, a value called conversion efficiency (e). The growth rate, ignoring mortality, is given by esPN. However, predators also have a natural death rate, which depends on their mortality rate (m) and their own population size (P). The complete equation for the predator population is:

dP/dt​=esPN−mP

Zero Isoclines and Population Cycles

To understand the dynamics of these populations, we can use zero isoclines, which are lines representing the conditions where a population's growth rate is zero (dN/dt=0 or dP/dt=0).

  • Prey Isocline: The prey population is stable (not growing or shrinking) when the predator population (P) is equal to a constant value, P=r/s. If the number of predators is below this line, prey numbers will increase. If predator numbers are above this line, prey numbers will decrease due to high predation pressure.
  • Predator Isocline: The predator population is stable when the prey population (N) is equal to a constant value, N=m/es. If the number of prey is to the left of this line (too few prey), predator numbers will decrease due to starvation. If the number of preys is to the right of this line (sufficient prey), predator numbers will increase.

The Combined Model and Oscillations

When these two zero isoclines are combined, they show a cyclical relationship between the predator and prey populations. This creates a pattern of coupled oscillations over time:

  1. When both predator and prey numbers are moderate, both populations grow.
  2. The increasing predator population puts intense pressure on the prey, causing the prey population to decline.
  3. The lack of prey leads to a decrease in the predator population due to starvation.
  4. With fewer predators, the prey population begins to rebound.
  5. Finally, the increase in prey allows the predator population to recover, starting the cycle over again.

The original Lotka-Volterra model predicts an endless cycle of predator and prey populations. This is driven by a time lag: predators increase in response to an increase in prey, which then causes the prey population to crash, leading to a subsequent crash in the predator population.

Refining the Lotka-Volterra Model

The Lotka-Volterra model is too simplistic for real-world scenarios, so it has been modified to be more accurate. These modified models incorporate additional factors that influence predator and prey populations.

  • Prey Isocline Modification: In the original model, the prey isocline is a straight, horizontal line. However, in reality, as prey density increases, intraspecific competition for resources also increases. This means that at a certain point, the prey population cannot grow further, even with low predator numbers. The modified prey isocline therefore bends downward, eventually hitting a carrying capacity (KN​) on the prey abundance axis.
  • Predator Isocline Modification: The predator isocline, which is a straight vertical line in the original model, is also modified. It now curves to the right because mutual interference among predators (e.g., fighting for territory) makes them less efficient hunters. This means a higher prey density is required to support the same number of predators. At a certain point, the predator population may level off because even an abundance of prey cannot support more predators due to other limiting factors.

Outcomes of the Modified Model

When these modified isoclines are combined, they intersect at a single stable point, leading to different outcomes than the endless cycles of the original model.

  • Damped Oscillations: The model can still show oscillations, but they are damped, meaning they become less severe over time until both populations stabilize.
  • No Oscillations: In some cases, especially when there is a high degree of mutual interference or territoriality among predators, the oscillations may disappear entirely, and the populations can reach a stable equilibrium without cycling.

These refinements show that the outcome of a predator-prey interaction---from endless cycles to complete stability---depends heavily on the specific behaviors and ecological factors of the species involved

Real-World Examples and Complex Dynamics

The Impact of Predator Diet

Another refinement shows that predator efficiency and diet diversity can drastically change the model's outcome. Predators that eat a variety of prey can sometimes drive a rare prey species to extinction. For example, if lions hunt both common wildebeest and rare sable antelope, they can sustain themselves on the wildebeest population while still heavily preying on the sable antelope. This "subsidized predation" means the lions can drive the rare antelope to extinction even though the antelope alone would not be enough to sustain the lion population.

Real-World Examples

There are real-world examples that demonstrate these complex dynamics. The classic 9- to 11-year cycle between the Canada lynx and snowshoe hare was initially thought to be a simple predator-prey oscillation. However, further research revealed that other factors are at play. As the hare population grows, it overgrazes its food plants, which then produce chemical defenses in response. This lack of quality food makes the hares weaker and more susceptible to predation, contributing to their population crash. The cycle is not just driven by predators but also by the availability of food for the prey, highlighting a three-level interaction (plant-herbivore-predator).

Additional Ecological Factors

Other ecological factors can also change the predator's carrying capacity and modify the isocline.

  • Super-predators or parasites can limit the predator population, independent of the prey population, creating a horizontal predator isocline.
  • The availability of alternate prey allows the predator population to increase even without an increase in the main prey population, shifting the isocline.
  • A straight predator isocline indicates that the predator population's carrying capacity is solely determined by the abundance of its primary prey.

Ultimately, while the Lotka-Volterra model provides a foundation, incorporating these real-world ecological and behavioral factors is essential for accurately predicting predator-prey dynamics.

Humans as Predators

Humans are apex predators capable of hunting species to extinction. This raises the question of whether we can harvest animal populations without causing long-term harm. The concept of Maximum Sustainable Yield (MSY) is a key ecological and economic model designed to address this. MSY is the largest number of individuals that can be removed from a population without causing a lasting decline. This is analogous to living off the interest of a bank account without touching the principal.

According to the logistic model of population growth, the maximum population increase occurs when the population is at half of its carrying capacity (K/2). Therefore, the MSY is estimated as MSY=rK/2, where r is the population's growth rate and K is its carrying capacity. This model suggests that to maximize yield, we should harvest populations when they are growing fastest, not when they are at their highest density.

Why Sustainable Fishing Is Difficult

Despite this simple model, maintaining a sustainable fishery is challenging for several reasons:

  • Difficulty in Estimating Population Size: It's hard for biologists to accurately count fish populations underwater, leading to overestimates of the sustainable harvest.
  • Technological Advances: Improvements in fishing gear over time mean that the same amount of effort yields larger catches, leading to overexploitation.
  • Economic Pressures: Economic models show that the maximum profit from a fishery is achieved at a level of effort below the MSY. However, commercial fisheries often operate at a level of effort that exceeds the MSY, leading to overfishing and population collapse. A notable example is the Canadian cod fishery, which collapsed in the early 1990s and has not been economically viable since.
  • Recreational Fishing Impact: Recreational fishing can also have a major impact. In the US, recreational landings accounted for a significant portion of the total catch for species of concern, such as red snapper in the Gulf of Mexico and red drum in the South Atlantic.

The Consequences of Overfishing

Overfishing can have devastating consequences, leading to the collapse of entire industries and long-term changes to ecosystems.

  • Peruvian Anchovy Fishery: One of the most famous examples is the collapse of the Peruvian anchovy fishery in the 1970s, which was the world's largest at the time. The collapse was caused by a combination of overfishing and an El Niño event that reduced the nutrient-rich waters the anchovies relied on. The fishery was suspended for 16 years, and it took another five years for the population to fully recover.
  • Changes in Fish Size and Species: A study of historical photographs from the Florida Keys documented a significant decline in the average size of fish caught from 1956 to 2007. The species composition also shifted from large-bodied fish like groupers and sharks to smaller fish like snappers. This suggests that overfishing targets larger fish, leading to a long-term reduction in average fish size within a community.

Whaling: A Historical Perspective

The history of whaling is a clear illustration of unsustainable human predation. As one whale species was depleted, whalers would move on to the next, less desirable one. In the Antarctic, the industry started by targeting the massive blue whales, then moved on to fin whales, humpback whales, and finally the smaller sei whales and minke whales as the larger species were depleted.

The International Whaling Commission (IWC) eventually voted for a moratorium on commercial whaling in 1982, which took effect in 1988. This protection has allowed some whale populations to begin to recover, with blue whale numbers off the California coast quadrupling in the 1980s and gray whale populations recovering to pre-whaling levels. This recovery demonstrates the powerful impact that reducing human predation can have on a species.
