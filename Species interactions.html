SPECIES INTERACTIONS

Species interactions are a fundamental aspect of ecology, shaping the dynamics of populations within a community. These interactions can be categorized based on their effects on the populations involved. Competition is a negative interaction for both species (-/-), as they vie for limited resources. In contrast, facilitation describes interactions where at least one species benefits. This includes mutualism (+/+), where both species benefit, and commensalism (+/0), where one benefits and the other is unaffected. Another significant category is natural enemies (+/-), which includes predation, herbivory, and parasitism, all of which benefit one species at the expense of another. Less common or studied interactions include amensalism (-/0), where one species is harmed and the other is unaffected, and neutralism (0/0), where there is no measurable interaction between species. Understanding these diverse relationships, such as those between rabbits and their competitors, predators, or parasites, is crucial for comprehending the factors that influence population densities and the overall structure of ecological systems.

Competition

Competition is a negative interaction (-/-) among two organisms. Several different types of competition are found in nature. Competition may be intraspecific, between individuals of the same species, or interspecific, between individuals of different species.

Types of Competition

Species competition, a fundamental ecological interaction, can be further broken down into three main types: exploitation competition, interference competition and apparent competition.

1. Exploitation Competition

Exploitation competition is an indirect form of competition where organisms deplete a shared resource, making it less available for others. The competitors never have to physically encounter each other to be in competition.

  • Plants competing for light: In a forest, tall trees and smaller plants both require sunlight for photosynthesis. The tall trees' canopies block the sun, reducing the amount of light that reaches the smaller plants on the forest floor. The smaller plants' growth is negatively affected, not because the trees are physically attacking them, but because the trees' use of a limited resource (light) has made that resource scarce for them.
  • Fly maggots in a carcass: A swarm of fly maggots feeding on a dead animal is a classic example. Each maggot consumes part of the carcass, reducing the total food available to every other maggot. The maggots are competing, but they are not fighting each other; their competition is a result of their collective consumption of a limited food supply.
  • Diurnal and nocturnal species: A species of deer that forages for berries during the day and a species of nocturnal raccoon that forages for the same berries at night are in exploitation competition. They never cross paths, but the deer's consumption of berries during the day leaves fewer for the raccoons at night, and vice versa.

Interference Competition

Interference competition involves direct, aggressive interactions where one organism actively prevents another from accessing a resource. This can include physical force, intimidation, or even chemical warfare.

  • Male deer fighting for mates: During the mating season, or "rut," male deer often engage in intense fights, locking horns to establish dominance. The winner gets access to the females and the opportunity to reproduce, while the loser is excluded. The resource is the mate, and the competition is direct and physical.
  • Lions and hyenas at a kill: When a lion pride has made a kill, they will often aggressively defend it against scavenging hyenas. The hyenas may try to steal the carcass, and the lions will use intimidation and physical force to chase them away. This is a direct struggle over a shared food source.
  • Allelopathy in plants: Some plants produce biochemicals (allelopathic compounds) that are released into the soil and inhibit the growth of neighboring plants. The plant is directly harming its competitors by poisoning their environment, thereby securing more resources like water and nutrients for itself.

Apparent competition

Apparent competition is a type of indirect interaction where two species don't compete for the same resources but instead share a common enemy, such as a predator or a parasite. This can often lead to one species being outcompeted by the other.A clear example involves the white-tailed deer and moose, which are both hosts to the meningeal worm, Parelaphostrongylus tenuis. While the white-tailed deer is largely unaffected by the worm, it is a devastating parasite for moose, causing severe neurological damage and even death. Because white-tailed deer are tolerant carriers, they can act as a reservoir for the parasite, effectively increasing the risk of infection for moose. This dynamic makes the white-tailed deer an "apparent competitor" with moose; their presence and high tolerance to the worm indirectly harm the moose population. As a result, moose are often not found in areas where white-tailed deer are common.

Apparent competition describes an indirect relationship where two different prey species negatively affect each other by supporting the same predator. They aren't competing for the same resources; instead, their population sizes influence the predator population, which in turn affects the other prey species.

Apparent competition is a fascinating ecological phenomenon where two prey species, even without competing for shared resources, indirectly influence each other's population dynamics through their shared predator. Consider a system with hawks preying on both squirrels and mice. When the squirrel population increases, it provides a more abundant food source for the hawks. This surge in food allows the hawk population to grow, leading to a larger predator population. While the mice may initially benefit from the hawks focusing on the more numerous squirrels, the long-term effect is often negative. If the squirrel population later declines, the now-larger hawk population will exert increased predation pressure on the remaining prey, including the mice. This heightened hunting pressure can lead to a significant drop in the mouse population. The opposite effect also holds true: a decline in the squirrel population means less food for the hawks, causing them to shift their predation efforts toward the mice, a more readily available food source. This increased hunting pressure from the hawks can then cause the mouse population to decline. Essentially, the fate of the two prey species is intertwined not through a struggle for food or habitat, but through their role as a food source for a common predator. Identifying this indirect interaction in the wild can be difficult, as it is often hidden within complex food webs and subject to continuous environmental shifts.

Associational susceptibility is a specific type of apparent competition that occurs in plants, where a herbivore "spills over" from its preferred host species to a neighboring one, causing damage to the secondary host.

For example, a study in northern Utah found that fall cankerworms, a type of caterpillar, prefer to eat box elder trees and are rarely found on isolated cottonwood trees. However, when cottonwoods grow near box elder trees, the cankerworms will spill over and feed on them, causing increased defoliation of the cottonwoods. While this interaction doesn't kill the cottonwoods, it demonstrates how the presence of a preferred food source (the box elder) can indirectly lead to a negative impact on a neighboring species (the cottonwood) by attracting a shared herbivore.

Amensalism

Sometimes, competition between species is not a balanced relationship. Instead of a negative impact on both species (-/-), one species is harmed while the other is unaffected. This is known as amensalism (-/0), and a common example is allelopathy, where one plant species secretes chemicals to inhibit the growth of another. A prime example is the black walnut tree, which produces a chemical called juglone from its roots. This chemical is toxic to the roots of many neighboring plants, killing them off and allowing the walnut tree to thrive without competition. This same principle is at play in the medical world with penicillin, a chemical derived from a fungus that kills bacteria. The fungus is unaffected, while the bacteria are eliminated. Invasive species also use allelopathy to their advantage. For instance, the South African bitou bush, introduced in Australia to stabilize sand dunes, secretes volatile compounds into the soil. These compounds inhibit the growth of native Australian plants, including the dominant acacia, allowing the bitou bush to spread more easily.

The Outcome of Competition can be Variable

The outcome of competition between species is not always fixed; it can change based on both living (biotic) and non-living (abiotic) factors in the environment.

a. The Influence of Biotic Factors

In a classic series of experiments, biologist Thomas Park studied competition between two species of flour beetles, Tribolium castaneum and Tribolium confusum. Initially, he found that T. confusum almost always won. However, it was later discovered that the beetle cultures were infected with a parasite, Adelina triboli, that was more lethal to T. castaneum. When the parasite was removed, the competitive outcome shifted, and T. castaneum became the more successful species. This showed how the presence of a parasite, a biotic factor, could completely alter the results of competition, a phenomenon now recognized as apparent competition. The experiments also revealed that even under controlled conditions, the outcome wasn't always predictable, a concept known as stochasticity.

b. The Influence of Abiotic Factors

Park also manipulated the abiotic environment, specifically temperature and moisture. He found that the competitive ability of each species was highly dependent on climate. T. confusum tended to win in dry conditions, while T. castaneum was more successful in wet conditions. Interestingly, T. confusum also dominated in cold and wet environments. The mechanism behind this competition was later found to be predation, with the beetles preying on the eggs and pupae of the other species.

These experiments demonstrated that the results of competition are not absolute. They are influenced by a combination of factors, including parasites, temperature, and moisture. Park's work highlights the importance of considering multiple environmental factors when studying species interactions, even in controlled settings, and suggests that competition is a common and powerful force in nature that can lead to the local extinction of some species.

Interspecific Competition is More Frequent in Nature

To determine if competition is a significant factor in nature, scientists often use a direct approach: they remove one species from a natural environment and observe the effect on the other. This method has been widely used and has provided strong evidence for the commonness of competition.

For example, a classic study on barnacles on the west coast of Scotland showed how two species, Balanus and Chthamalus, compete for space. By experimentally removing one species, researchers were able to see how the other was affected.

In broader reviews of field studies, researchers Joseph Connell and Thomas Schoener independently found that competition is indeed frequent in nature, occurring in 55% and 75% of the surveyed species, respectively. Connell's review also revealed a key pattern: competition is almost always observed (90%) in studies of just two species sharing a resource, but this frequency drops to 50% in studies with more than two species. This is because competition is often strongest between species that are most similar in their resource use. For instance, in a system where ants, beetles, mice, and birds feed on grains of different sizes, competition is most likely to occur between "adjacent" species like beetles and mice, rather than between more distantly related species like ants and birds. This pattern demonstrates that the likelihood of competition is directly related to the number of species and the degree of overlap in their resource use.

Reviews of field studies by Connell and Schoener also uncovered some general patterns regarding which types of organisms compete most. They found that plants exhibit a high degree of competition, possibly because their stationary nature prevents them from moving away from competitors, forcing them to vie for the same limited resources like water, light, and minerals. Similarly, marine organisms, particularly those in the intertidal zone, showed more competition than terrestrial species. This is likely because many of these organisms are also sessile, or attached to a limited rock surface, making space a highly contested and important resource.

Guild Concept in Ecology

In ecology, the concept of a guild helps us understand where competition is most likely to occur. Coined by ecologist Dick Root, a guild is a group of different species that share the same resource and consume it in a similar way. This is a more specific way of classifying species than just by their diet; it focuses on their feeding strategy. For example, a community of birds might contain several different guilds. A leaf-gleaning guild consists of species that all forage for insects on the surface of leaves, while a ground-feeding guild includes species that search for worms and other invertebrates on the forest floor. Similarly, a single plant might be a host to multiple insect guilds: a leaf-chewing guild, a stem-feeding guild, and a root-feeding guild. Because all members of a guild are exploiting the exact same resource in the same manner, competition among them is highly probable. This is especially true when the resource is limited or spatially confined. A compelling example is the stem-boring guild of insects that live on salt marsh cordgrass. This guild is made up of five different species, all of which bore into the plant's stems to feed on the tissue inside. The narrow, enclosed space of the stems leads to frequent encounters between the insects. Ecologists Peter Stiling and Donald Strong documented this intense competition, observing the species directly fighting one another with their sharp mandibles, with conflicts often ending in death. This illustrates how the shared feeding method and limited space within a guild can lead to severe and direct competition.

Testing Competition Theory

Ecologists Ian Kaplan and Robert Denno sought to test the central tenets of traditional competition theory. They identified three key predictions that suggest competition is most intense between organisms that are most alike.

  • Intraspecific competition should be more severe than interspecific competition, since resource overlap is greater.
  • The strength of interspecific competition is greater between more closely related species or species in similar guilds.
  • The strength of interspecific competition increases with decreased resources

The first prediction is that intraspecific competition—the competition among individuals of the same species—should be more severe than interspecific competition, as members of the same species have nearly identical resource needs. The second prediction posits that the strength of competition between different species is directly related to their similarity; in other words, competition is stronger between more closely related species or those that belong to the same guild (a group of species that use the same resources in a similar way). The final prediction states that competition intensifies as resources become scarce.

To test these hypotheses, Kaplan and Denno performed a large-scale meta-analysis of data from 145 different studies on plant-eating insects. They chose these insects because they are well-suited for experimental manipulation and their resource use is easily observed. The researchers analyzed data from experiments where one species was removed to see how it affected a competing species. By examining a range of variables—including growth rate, body size, survival, and reproductive success—they were able to quantify the overall effect of competition and determine if the predictions of traditional theory held true.

Invasive Species and Competition

Human activity, whether intentional or accidental, has introduced thousands of nonnative species worldwide. Many of these invasive species have become pests, often outcompeting native species for resources and displacing them. The following examples highlight how this has occurred across different groups of organisms.

a. Vertebrates

On Anticosti Island in Canada, the introduction of white-tailed deer led to the extinction of the native black bear population. The deer, with no natural predators, rapidly multiplied and over-consumed shrubs, dramatically reducing the number of berries—a critical food source for the bears to build up energy for winter. This exemplifies how an introduced competitor can indirectly starve a native species to extinction. In Australia, introduced placental mammals have caused similar problems. The European rabbit, for instance, competes with native burrowing animals like the boodie and the rabbit-eared bandicoot. Introduced dingoes are believed to have contributed to the extinction of the thylacine, a marsupial wolf, on mainland Australia. Sheep introduced for the wool industry compete with various kangaroo species, including the red kangaroo and the western gray kangaroo. In New Zealand, introduced red deer and feral goats compete with the native kokako bird for food, threatening its survival. The problem extends to aquatic environments, as seen in California's freshwater systems, where 24 of 26 well-studied nonnative fish species negatively impact native fish. A prime example is the cichlid fish introduced to Gatun Lake, Panama, which eliminated six of the eight native fish species within five years. In the Great Lakes, the invasive alewife displaced much of the native fish fauna by competing for food.

b. Invertebrates

Over 1,500 insect species have been introduced to the continental U.S., some with devastating effects. The red imported fire ant, accidentally introduced from Argentina, has spread rapidly across the southeastern U.S., outcompeting many native ant species and altering habitats with their large nests. In freshwater systems, invasive mussels and crayfish are also known to outcompete native invertebrates.

c. Plants

Invasive plants are a global issue. In U.S. wetlands, purple loosestrife chokes out native plants, which in turn endangers species like ducks and turtles that rely on them for food and shelter. The Chinese tallow tree and garlic mustard have created vast monocultures in the southern and eastern U.S., respectively. In California, barb goatgrass thrives on nutrient-poor, toxic soils, outcompeting native species that cannot tolerate these conditions. In the Florida Everglades, Brazilian pepper, Australian pine, and especially the punk-tree are suffocating native vegetation. Kudzu vine, introduced to the southeastern states, smothers large areas of native habitat.

General Trends and Hypotheses

A review of 55 case studies confirmed that competition is a common interaction between invasive and native species, with invasives having a negative effect on natives in most cases (75% of the time). Although some native species can successfully suppress colonizers, invasive species often gain a competitive advantage. Ecologists have proposed several hypotheses to explain this:

  • The Enemy Release Hypothesis: Invasives thrive because they are free from the predators, pathogens, and parasites that kept their populations in check in their native habitats, allowing them to allocate more energy to growth and reproduction.
  • The Superior Competitor Hypothesis: Some invasive species are simply more efficient at acquiring and using resources than native species.
  • The Propagule Pressure Hypothesis: Invasives produce a large number of offspring, and their sheer numbers allow them to overwhelm and outcompete native species.
  • The Lack of Environmental Constraints Hypothesis: Some invasive species are simply pre-adapted to the new environment, allowing them to flourish without the challenges faced by native species.

Competition may occur between biological control agents

Agricultural pest control relies on two main methods: chemical control using pesticides and biological control, which involves introducing natural enemies to manage pests. Biological control is often preferred because it avoids the environmental pollution associated with chemical residues. A common, but potentially flawed, strategy in biocontrol is to release multiple enemy species at once, hoping one will effectively control the pest.

Research by Les Ehler and R. W. Hall suggests this strategy may be counterproductive. They compared the establishment rates of natural enemies released to control various pests, including sap-sucking bugs (Homoptera), butterflies and moths (Lepidoptera), and beetles (Coleoptera). Their findings showed that the success rate of establishing a viable enemy population dropped significantly when multiple species were released simultaneously. For instance, single-species releases had a 76% success rate, while the rate dropped to just 50% when two or more species were released together.

This data indicates that competition among the introduced species could be a contributing factor to the failure of some biological control campaigns. While the study doesn't definitively prove competition, its results are consistent with the idea that introduced natural enemies can compete with each other, hindering their overall effectiveness.

Competition Between Biological Control Agents

Research provides strong evidence that competition occurs between biological control agents. A classic example is the sequential introduction of three parasitic wasps to control the red scale (Aonidiella aurantii), an insect pest on orange trees in Southern California. The red scale is a small insect that sucks sap from citrus plants, and its feeding leads to the growth of sooty mold, which devalues the fruit.

Many of the red scale's enemies are parasitic wasps that lay their eggs inside the scale insects. The wasp larvae then consume the scale from the inside out. The first wasp, Aphytis chrysomphali, was accidentally introduced around 1900 but was not very effective at controlling the red scale. In 1948, a second species, A. lignanensis, was introduced from South China and began to displace A. chrysomphali in various regions like Santa Barbara and Orange counties. However, the red scale remained a problem.

The situation changed in 1956 when a third wasp, A. melinus, was imported from India. This species immediately displaced A. lignanensis in hotter, inland areas such as the San Fernando Valley. The outcome, however, was not the same everywhere, as A. lignanensis remained dominant in the milder coastal habitat of Santa Barbara, demonstrating how climate can influence competitive outcomes.

The reason for this competitive displacement was that the female A. melinus wasps were able to use smaller scale insects as hosts and lay a greater number of eggs. This gave them a reproductive advantage over the other species. The introduction of A. melinus proved to be a major success, as it provided effective control of the red scale in the inland areas. This case study illustrates how competition between biological control agents can occur and how it can be influenced by environmental factors. It also shows that the introduction of a superior competitor can sometimes be beneficial for pest control.

Competition may occur between biological control agents and native species

While biological control agents are introduced to manage pests, they can sometimes negatively impact native species through competition. A well-documented example involves the seven-spot ladybird beetle (Coccinella septempunctata), which was introduced to North America from Europe to control aphids. This introduction had unintended consequences for the native nine-spot ladybird beetle (Coccinella novemnotata).

Before the introduction of the seven-spot ladybird, the nine-spot ladybird was a common species. However, the seven-spot ladybird proved to be a superior competitor for the same food source—aphids. It was more efficient at finding and consuming aphids, and it also preyed on the larvae of the native nine-spot ladybird. As a result, the population of the nine-spot ladybird declined dramatically, and it is now considered rare in many parts of North America.

This case demonstrates that the introduction of a non-native biological control agent can lead to competition with native species, potentially causing their decline. It highlights the importance of carefully considering the potential ecological impacts before introducing any new species for pest control.
