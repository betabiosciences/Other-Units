THE COMPETITIVE EXCLUSION PRINCIPLE

(GAUSE'S LAW)

Definition and Core Tenet

The Competitive Exclusion Principle, frequently referred to as Gause's Law, is a foundational proposition in ecology. It asserts that two species competing for the exact same limited resource cannot coexist indefinitely at constant population values.

This principle is often encapsulated by the maxim: "complete competitors cannot coexist". The implication is that even if one species possesses only a slight advantage in acquiring or utilizing a shared resource, it will eventually dominate. This dominance can lead either to the local extinction of the competitively weaker species or compel that species to undergo an evolutionary or behavioral shift towards occupying a different ecological niche.

Historical Context and Key Proponents

While the principle is classically attributed to Georgy Gause, it is noteworthy that he did not explicitly formulate it as a universal law in its modern phrasing. The conceptual underpinnings of competitive exclusion can be traced back to Charles Darwin's theory of natural selection, where the struggle for existence inherently implies competitive outcomes.

Joseph Grinnell, an American naturalist, articulated an early version of the principle based on field observations in 1904, stating: "Two species of approximately the same food habits are not likely to remain long evenly balanced in numbers in the same region. One will crowd out the other".

Georgy Gause's significant contribution in the 1930s was to provide rigorous laboratory experimental validation, which cemented the principle's scientific standing.

Classic Experimental Evidence: Gause's Paramecium Experiments

Gause's groundbreaking studies on the effects of interspecific competition utilized two closely related species of single-celled ciliates, Paramecium aurelia and Paramecium caudatum. Both species primarily feed on bacteria, yeast, and algae.

In his experimental design, Gause first grew each Paramecium species in separate culture tubes, providing a consistent daily supply of bacteria and yeast as food along with fresh water. This allowed him to observe their individual population growth dynamics. Subsequently, he introduced both species into the same culture tube, maintaining identical and constant environmental conditions, including food and water supply. The constancy of these ecological factors was a critical aspect of his experimental setup, as the competitive exclusion principle is understood to apply most strictly under such stable conditions.

Experimental Results

Individual Growth

When cultured individually, both Paramecium species thrived, exhibiting logistic population growth patterns.

Growth Rates

Paramecium aurelia demonstrated a faster growth rate and achieved higher population densities compared to Paramecium caudatum.

Competition Outcome

When grown together, both species initially increased in number, but Paramecium caudatum eventually declined and became extinct.

Competitive Advantage

Paramecium aurelia consistently outcompeted Paramecium caudatum for the shared food resources.

Gause attributed this outcome to the requirement for "but a single niche" under the constant conditions of his experiment. It is interesting to note that Paramecium caudatum is approximately four times the size of Paramecium aurelia, a size difference that could have played a role in the competitive dynamics. Importantly, Gause also demonstrated that Paramecium caudatum could survive if the environmental parameters, such as food or water availability, were varied. This finding underscored the strict conditions under which competitive exclusion operates, highlighting its dependence on environmental constancy.

Other Experimental and Theoretical Support

The competitive exclusion principle is not solely supported by Gause's pioneering work. It is robustly predicted by various mathematical and theoretical models, including the Lotka-Volterra models of competition, which describe the population dynamics of competing species and predict conditions under which competitive exclusion is likely to occur. Beyond Gause, other laboratory experiments, such as those conducted by Park (1948, 1954, 1962) on two species of flour beetle, Tribolium confusum and Tribolium castaneum, have further illustrated the principle.

In applied ecology, the competitive exclusion principle finds modern utility in pest control strategies like the Sterile Insect Technique (SIT). This technique involves the release of sterile individuals into a population to outcompete wild populations for mating opportunities, thereby leading to a reduction or eradication of the target pest population. This practical application demonstrates the principle's predictive power in real-world scenarios.

Real-World Examples

Several instances in natural systems appear to exemplify competitive exclusion. A prominent example is the replacement of the native red squirrel by the introduced grey squirrel in Britain. The decline of the red squirrel population is attributed to a combination of factors, including direct competitive exclusion for resources, disease transmission, and differing abilities to utilize available food sources like hazelnuts. In other ecosystems, the competitive exclusion exerted by voles has been observed to influence the amount and quality of resources accessible to deer mice, consequently shaping patterns of ecological succession in those communities.

Limitations and the "Paradox of the Plankton"

Despite its strong theoretical foundation and experimental validation in controlled settings, competitive exclusion is surprisingly rarely observed as a direct cause of extinction in complex natural ecosystems. Many biological communities appear to defy Gause's law, maintaining high levels of biodiversity even when species share seemingly similar resource requirements.

A classic illustration of this apparent contradiction is the "paradox of the plankton." Numerous plankton species coexist in aquatic environments, despite relying on a very limited set of fundamental resources, primarily solar energy and dissolved minerals in the water. According to the competitive exclusion principle, only a small number of plankton species should be able to coexist under such resource limitations.

This discrepancy suggests that natural conditions are seldom constant enough for direct competitive exclusion to run its full course. The principle, while powerful, serves more as a fundamental selective pressure in nature rather than a universally observed outcome of outright extinction. Its limitations highlight the dynamic interplay of environmental variability and species adaptations that actively prevent outright exclusion, thus promoting biodiversity.

The intensity of interspecific competition is closely linked to the functional similarity between species. Hypotheses such as the competitive-relatedness hypothesis and the phylogenetic limiting similarity hypothesis propose that competition is most intense among species that possess similar functional traits and therefore compete for similar resources and habitats. The consequence of such intense competition, if it leads to exclusion, is a reduction in the number of closely related species and a more even distribution of species across functional trait space, a pattern known as phylogenetic overdispersion. This suggests a deeper connection between the micro-level competitive interactions and the macro-level patterns of community assembly and evolutionary diversification. If competitive exclusion acts as a filter, eliminating species that are too similar, then the species that successfully coexist in a community should, on average, be less functionally or phylogenetically similar than expected by chance. This indicates that competition acts as an evolutionary force, shaping the functional and phylogenetic diversity of ecosystems by favoring species that are sufficiently distinct to avoid direct competitive overlap.
