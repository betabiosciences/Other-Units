THE SPECIES RICHNESS AND COMMUNITY SERVICES

The health and sustainability of human society are fundamentally linked to the species richness of the planet's natural ecosystems, which is simply the count of different species within a community. High species richness is a key component of biodiversity and is strongly associated with an ecosystem's functional capacity and resilience to stress, with more diverse systems being better able to maintain productivity and recover from disturbances like disease or extreme weather. This biological abundance is the essential driver behind ecosystem services, which are the vital, life-sustaining benefits nature provides to humanity. These services are traditionally grouped into three categories: provisioning services (such as food, fresh water, and medicinal resources), regulating services (like climate control, water purification, and pollination), and cultural services (such as recreational and spiritual enrichment). Recognizing the foundational role of species richness in delivering these invaluable services highlights a critical imperative: preserving the variety of life is not merely an environmental goal, but an economic and social necessity for human well-being.

Hypotheses on Species Richness and Community Services

Ecologists have long debated the relationship between the number of species (species richness) and how well an ecological community functions.

1. Diversity-stability hypothesis

One of the earliest and most influential ideas, put forth by Charles Elton in the 1950s, was the diversity-stability hypothesis. This hypothesis posits that a community with more species is inherently more stable. If we consider stability to be a measure of overall community performance or the quality of its community services (the benefits the ecosystem provides), Elton's idea suggests a direct, linear correlation: as the number of species increases, the delivery of community services rises proportionally.

2. Rivet Hypothesis

In 1981, Paul and Ann Ehrlich proposed the rivet hypothesis to explain the link between species loss and ecosystem function. This model uses the analogy of an airplane, where each species is like a rivet holding the plane (the community) together.

Initially, the loss of one or a few species (rivets) may not visibly harm the system, as the community can tolerate some redundancy. However, the Ehrlichs argued that we cannot know in advance which species are most critical. As species richness continues to decline, the community's ability to provide services (its airworthiness) is gradually but significantly impaired. The overall function doesn't fail all at once; instead, community services are predicted to decline in a stepwise fashion, with tipping points where the loss of just a few more species causes a disproportionately large and potentially critical failure in the ecosystem.

3. Redundancy Hypothesis

Brian Walker proposed the redundancy hypothesis in 1992, which suggests that many species within a community have overlapping or identical roles. In this model, species are considered redundant because the loss of one can be compensated for by others that perform the same essential function. Consequently, the hypothesis predicts that species loss generally has no observable effect on community services or overall ecosystem function, provided the lost species was not one of the few that performs a unique and critically important function. Essentially, the community has a functional buffer built into its diversity.

4. Keystone hypothesis

The keystone hypothesis offers a contrasting view, arguing that the function of an entire community rests disproportionately on a few, critically important species. This model suggests that community services plummet immediately once a keystone species—a member that plays an indispensable role—is removed, even if overall species richness has only declined slightly from its natural state. Therefore, this hypothesis predicts that even minimal species loss can lead to an immediate and significant reduction in the ecosystem's ability to provide services.

5. Idiosyncratic Hypothesis

The final alternative is the idiosyncratic hypothesis, which proposes that while changes in species richness do affect community services, the direction and magnitude of these changes are unpredictable. According to this view, the specific roles and interactions of individual species are so unique and complex that the net effect on ecosystem function cannot be reliably modeled or forecasted. Understanding which of these five models accurately describes how natural communities respond to species loss is crucial, as this knowledge fundamentally informs and dictates how we approach environmental management and conservation.

Research Findings

Experimental and field analyses support the idea that species-rich communities perform better than species-poor communities. These reports also support the redundancy hypothesis, in which the community function is impaired only when species richness is greatly reduced.

Experimental studies also suggest that species richness affects community function at higher trophic levels as well as lower trophic levels.

Increased plant species richness increases herbivore species richness, which in turn, increases predator species richness. High predator species richness is more effective in reducing herbivore abundance and increasing plant growth.

On coral reefs, overfishing causes the density of coral-feeding butterfly fishes to increase, by competitive release, and increased butterfly-fish feeding in turn spreads the coral disease.

Reviews recommend that species-rich communities perform much better than species-poor communities, particularly because of the sampling effect, not species complementarity. Communities are stable when their species richness changes very little over time.
