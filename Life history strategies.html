LIFE HISTORY STRATEGIES

Introduction to Life History Strategies

Life history strategies are a set of evolved traits that influence an organism's survival, reproduction, and overall fitness. These strategies are not conscious choices but rather adaptive features shaped by natural selection in response to environmental pressures. They encompass a wide range of characteristics, including reproductive patterns, lifespan, competitive ability, and habitat preference. Understanding these strategies is crucial for predicting how populations grow and respond to changes in their environment.

Reproductive Timing: Semelparity vs. Iteroparity

One of the most fundamental aspects of a life history strategy is the timing of reproduction. Organisms can either reproduce once and die, a strategy known as semelparity, or reproduce multiple times throughout their lives, a strategy called iteroparity.

  • Semelparity: The term comes from Latin words meaning "once to bear." This strategy involves a single, often massive, reproductive event before death. It's common in many insects, annual plants, and even some larger organisms like Pacific salmon and agave plants. The trade-off here is clear: the organism dedicates all its energy to a single burst of reproduction, maximizing the number of offspring but at the cost of its own survival. This strategy is often favoured in environments where the survival of offspring is highly unpredictable, as it's a form of "bet-hedging"---a large number of seeds or eggs increases the chance that at least some will survive.
  • Iteroparity: This strategy, from the Latin "to repeat," involves repeated reproductive cycles over a lifetime. It is the norm for most vertebrates and perennial plants, such as trees. Iteroparity can be further divided:
    • Seasonal Iteroparity: Reproduction occurs during specific, favourable seasons. This is typical for animals in temperate climates, such as deer and many bird species, as well as plants like temperate forest trees.
    • Continuous Iteroparity: Reproduction can happen year-round. This is more common in stable, tropical environments and among some primates and parasites.

Semelparity vs. Iteroparity Comparison

Feature Semelparity Iteroparity
Reproductive Frequency Single, once-in-a-lifetime event Multiple reproductive events over a lifetime
Lifespan after Reproduction The organism dies shortly after reproduction The organism survives and can reproduce again
Investment per Offspring Lower investment per offspring (often many small ones) Higher investment per offspring (often fewer, larger ones)
Number of Offspring Often a very large number in a single event Varies, but total number over lifetime can be high
Environmental Context Favored in unpredictable environments (bet-hedging) Favored in stable, predictable environments
Examples Agave plants, Pacific salmon, annual insects and plants Most vertebrates (mammals, birds, reptiles), perennial trees

Iteroparity is a safer bet in stable environments, allowing organisms to allocate energy to both reproduction and self-maintenance. By living longer and breeding multiple times, they increase the probability of leaving behind successful offspring, especially if conditions are not ideal in a particular year.

The r/K Selection Theory

Developed by Robert MacArthur and Edward Wilson, the r/K selection theory provides a framework for understanding how life history strategies are linked to population growth and competition. The theory views strategies as a continuum between two extremes:

  • r-Selected Species: These species are characterized by a high intrinsic rate of population increase (r). They prioritize rapid growth and large numbers of offspring over competitive ability. They are often small, mature quickly, have short lifespans, and produce many tiny offspring. Examples include weeds, bacteria, and insects. These organisms thrive in disturbed or unpredictable environments, quickly colonizing new habitats before they are outcompeted by other species.
  • K-Selected Species: These species are adapted to stable environments where populations are at or near the carrying capacity (K) of their habitat. They are strong competitors and prioritize quality over quantity. They tend to be large, mature slowly, live for a long time, and produce few, large offspring. Examples include oak trees, elephants, and large carnivores. Their traits, such as longevity and large size, give them a competitive edge in mature, stable ecosystems. However, these very traits make them particularly vulnerable to human-induced disturbances like habitat loss and overhunting, as their populations cannot recover quickly from a decline.

Grime's Triangle: An Alternative Perspective

Ecologist Phillip Grime proposed a different model, the Grime's triangle, to classify plant life history strategies based on three factors: stress, disturbance, and competition. The three corners of the triangle represent three primary strategies:

  1. Ruderals: These are disturbance-tolerant species, similar to r-selected species. They thrive in areas with frequent disturbance and are quick to colonize new ground.
  2. Competitors: These species are dominant in productive, low-stress environments where competition for resources is intense. They are excellent at outcompeting other plants.
  3. Stress Tolerators: These species are adapted to environments with high levels of stress (e.g., extreme temperatures, poor soil) where resources are scarce. They grow slowly and are efficient at surviving under difficult conditions.

Grime's model suggests a trade-off: a plant cannot be a superior competitor and a stress tolerator at the same time. Many species, however, fall somewhere in the middle, displaying a mix of these traits. This framework can also be applied to animals, with desert animals acting as stress tolerators and large carnivores as competitors.

A demographic interpretation of this model by Jonathan Silvertown and colleagues further clarifies the trade-offs in terms of fecundity (reproductive output), growth, and survival. This model shows that species can be successful by excelling in one or two of these areas, but not all three. For instance, a semelparous plant might have high fecundity but low survival, while an iteroparous tree has high survival but lower annual fecundity.

Population Viability Analysis (PVA)

Life history data is critical for conservation efforts, particularly through a technique called Population Viability Analysis (PVA). This method uses computer models to assess the probability that a population will go extinct over a given time period. It integrates various factors, including population size, age structure, and the effects of chance events like droughts or diseases.

A key output of a PVA is the Minimum Viable Population (MVP), which is the smallest population size needed to ensure a species' long-term survival. For example, a study using PVA on 102 vertebrate species concluded that an average of 7,316 sexually mature adults is needed to maintain a population for at least 40 generations. This powerful tool helps conservation biologists determine the necessary size of protected areas and develop effective management strategies to prevent extinction.
