GENETICS - BASIC TERMINOLOGIES

Fundamental Concepts

Genome

A complete set of chromosomal genes, inherited from parent or entire genotype of a cell or individual.

Gene (Mendel's factor)

The fundamental physical and functional unit of heredity, carries information from one generation to the next; a segment of DNA, composed of a transcribed region and a regulatory sequence, that makes possible transcription. Danish geneticist Wilhelm Johannsen first used it. Gene is a segment of a DNA molecule that codes for the synthesis of a single polypeptide; it is as an inherited factor that determines a characteristic.

Character or characteristics

A Character (or Characteristic) is a heritable attribute or feature of an organism that is selected for investigation and study in genetics. These attributes are often general categories that can have distinct forms. For example, plant height is a character, and the specific forms it takes—tall or dwarf—are the corresponding traits. Similarly, human skin color is a character, while various shades are the resulting phenotypes. Characters serve as the basis for tracking and analyzing the patterns of inheritance across generations.

Chromosome

Condensed chromatic structure in the cell nucleus that carry genes encoded by DNA.

Alleles and Chromosomal Organization

Allele (Allelomorph)

An alternative form of a gene that occurs at the same locus on homologous chromosomes. Alleles are genes controlling the same characteristics like hair colour but producing different effects (black/red) and occupying corresponding positions on homologous chromosome. e.g., R and r are alleles for seed shape . Thus, there is a specific place—a locus—on a chromosome in pea plants where the shape of seeds is determined. This locus might be occupied by an allele for round seeds (R) or one for wrinkled seeds (r).

Locus

The Locus (plural: Loci) is the specific, fixed position or physical location on a chromosome occupied by a particular gene or one of its alternative forms, called an allele. Since genes are arranged linearly along a chromosome, the locus provides a unique address for a gene. In a pair of homologous chromosomes, the loci for the same gene are located at the corresponding positions. For instance, the gene controlling human eye color always resides at the same locus on chromosome 15, though different individuals may have different alleles (e.g., for blue or brown color) occupying that spot.

Homologous chromosomes

Homologous Chromosomes are a pair of chromosomes that are identical in size, shape, and gene sequence, with one member of the pair inherited from the male parent (paternal homolog) and the other from the female parent (maternal homolog). They are the chromosomes that pair up during meiosis. While they contain genes for the same traits at the same loci (positions), they may carry different alleles (versions) of those genes. For instance, both homologs will carry the gene for eye color, but one might carry the allele for brown eyes, and the other for blue eyes. The pairing of homologous chromosomes is characteristic of all diploid organisms, ensuring that two complete sets of genetic information are present in every somatic cell.

Genetic Composition

Hybrid

A Hybrid is an individual organism produced from a cross, or mating, between two parents of differing genotypes. The term most commonly refers to an individual that is heterozygous for one or more genes being studied, meaning it possesses two different alleles at a particular locus (e.g., Aa). For instance, crossing a pure-breeding tall pea plant (TT) with a pure-breeding dwarf pea plant (tt) results in F1​ progeny that are all hybrids (Tt). More broadly, the term can also describe the offspring of a cross between two different species (like a mule, which is the hybrid of a horse and a donkey).

Homozygous and Heterozygous

The terms Homozygous and Heterozygous describe the genetic makeup, or genotype, of an individual with respect to a specific gene. Since most complex organisms are diploid, they possess two alleles for every gene, one inherited from each parent.

Homozygous

An individual is Homozygous for a trait when they possess two identical alleles for that gene at the corresponding loci on homologous chromosomes.

  • Homozygous Dominant: The genotype includes two copies of the dominant allele (e.g., TT for tallness). The individual will express the dominant phenotype.
  • Homozygous Recessive: The genotype includes two copies of the recessive allele (e.g., tt for dwarfness). The individual will express the recessive phenotype, as no dominant allele is present to mask it.

Heterozygous

An individual is Heterozygous for a trait when they possess two different alleles for that gene (e.g., Tt).

  • The genotype consists of one dominant allele and one recessive allele.
  • The individual will typically express the dominant phenotype, as the presence of the dominant allele masks the expression of the recessive one.
  • A heterozygous individual is also referred to as a hybrid or a carrier (in the case of recessive genetic disorders).

Dominance and Expression

Dominant and Recessive Alleles

The terms Dominant and Recessive Alleles describe how different versions of a gene interact to determine an organism's observable trait, or phenotype.

Dominant Allele

A Dominant Allele is one that masks or hides the expression of a recessive allele when both are present in a heterozygous individual. It exerts its effect regardless of the second allele it is paired with. A dominant allele is conventionally represented by an uppercase letter (e.g., R for round seeds).

  • If an organism has at least one dominant allele (i.e., the genotype is homozygous dominant (RR) or heterozygous (Rr)), the dominant phenotype will be expressed.

Recessive Allele

A Recessive Allele is one whose expression is masked by a dominant allele in the heterozygous state. It is only able to exert its effect—that is, the recessive phenotype is expressed—when the individual is homozygous recessive, meaning they possess two copies of the recessive allele. A recessive allele is conventionally represented by a lowercase letter (e.g., r for wrinkled seeds).

  • If an organism has a heterozygous genotype (Rr), the recessive allele (r) is present but is not expressed phenotypically.
  • The recessive phenotype appears only in the homozygous recessive genotype (rr).

Phenotype

The Phenotype is the observable appearance or manifestation of a genetic trait in an organism. It encompasses all detectable characteristics, whether they are physical (like having round seeds or a specific hair color), physiological (such as the ability to metabolize a certain sugar), biochemical (e.g., the structure of a protein), or behavioral. Unlike the genotype, which is the organism's set of genes, the phenotype is the expression of those genes, and its final form is the result of the interaction between the individual's genotype and the environment. For example, while a person's genes may code for a maximum height (genotype), their actual adult height (phenotype) can be influenced by environmental factors like nutrition.

Genotype

The Genotype is the specific combination of alleles an individual possesses for a particular gene, which fundamentally determines one of its characteristics or traits. It represents the inherited genetic constitution, acting as the blueprint for an organism's potential phenotypes. For example, for the trait of plant height, the possible genotypes are Homozygous Dominant (TT), Heterozygous (Tt), or Homozygous Recessive (tt). Critically, the genotype is inherited from parents to offspring, whereas the resulting phenotype (the observable trait) is a manifestation that can be further influenced by environmental factors.

Advanced Genetic Concepts

Crossing Over

Crossing Over is a pivotal event in genetics defined as the exchange of genetic material between the non-sister chromatids of homologous chromosomes. This physical exchange occurs during Prophase I of meiosis, when homologous chromosomes pair up in a structure called a bivalent, and a cross-shaped structure known as a chiasma forms. The primary importance of crossing over is that it breaks the physical linkage between alleles that were originally on the same chromosome, leading to the recombination of genes. This process shuffles the parental alleles into new combinations on the chromatids, significantly increasing the genetic variation in the resulting gametes and, subsequently, the offspring.

Wild type Vs Mutant Alleles

The contrast between Wild-Type Alleles and Mutant Alleles is fundamental to understanding genetic variation and disease.

Wild-Type Allele

A Wild-Type Allele is the allele most prevalent in a natural population, often defined as being present at a frequency greater than 1%. These alleles are typically considered the "normal" or standard form of the gene. They are crucial because they usually encode a protein that is produced in the correct amount and functions properly, thereby conferring a normal or common phenotype. In genetic notation, the wild-type allele is often denoted with a superscript plus sign (e.g., w+).

Mutant Allele

A Mutant Allele is any allele that has been altered from the wild-type by a mutation and is usually present at a frequency of less than 1% in a population. These alleles generally result in a change in the quantity or function of the encoded protein (e.g., a non-functional, reduced-function, or over-active protein). Mutant alleles are often responsible for genetic diseases or abnormal phenotypes and are most frequently inherited in a recessive fashion, meaning a single copy of the wild-type allele is usually sufficient to mask the effect of the mutant allele.

Breeding Systems

Filial Generation

The Filial Generation refers to the successive generations of offspring produced from a controlled genetic cross, a term derived from the Latin for "son" or "daughter." These generations are the core units of study in genetics, particularly in the foundational principles established by Gregor Mendel. The process begins with the Parental (P) Generation, which consists of the initial two individuals, typically chosen because they are true-breeding (homozygous) for opposing forms of a characteristic, such as dominant and recessive traits. When the P generation is crossed, their progeny form the First Filial (F1​) Generation. The F1​ generation is genetically uniform; in a simple monohybrid cross, all F1​ individuals are heterozygous and express only the dominant trait (e.g., all purple-flowered pea plants, Pp, from a PP×pp cross). The analysis continues with the Second Filial (F2​) Generation, which is produced by interbreeding (or self-pollinating) the F1​ individuals. The F2​ generation is of critical importance because it is where the traits hidden in the F1​ reappear, revealing the classical Mendelian ratios, such as the 3:1 phenotypic ratio and 1:2:1 genotypic ratio, which mathematically demonstrate the underlying rules of heredity and allele segregation.

Pure line (pure breeding Line)

The concept of a Pure Line (also called a pure-breeding line or true-breeding strain) describes a group of individuals that are homozygous for all genes being considered. This state of complete or near-complete homozygosity is typically achieved through multiple generations of self-pollination (in plants) or close inbreeding (in animals). Because they possess two identical alleles for each gene (e.g., AA or aa), a pure line is genetically uniform and will always "breed true," meaning they produce offspring with the exact same phenotype as the parents when crossed among themselves. Any minor variation observed in the traits of a pure line is non-heritable and purely the result of environmental influences. Gregor Mendel relied on these pure lines for his P (Parental) generation to ensure the precise starting genotypes necessary for observing the predictable patterns of inheritance.

Types of Genetic Crosses

Back cross

A Backcross is a genetic procedure where a hybrid individual, typically the F1​ generation offspring resulting from an initial cross, is mated back to one of its two original parental lines. The primary purpose of a backcross is to efficiently transfer a specific, desirable gene (such as one for disease resistance) from a Donor Parent into the genetic background of a superior, well-established Recurrent Parent. When the process is repeated over several generations (e.g., BC1​, BC2​, BC3​), it rapidly increases the proportion of the offspring's genome that is identical to the Recurrent Parent, while ensuring the retention of the single, target gene. A specific application of this concept is the Test Cross, a backcross where the F1​ individual with a dominant phenotype is crossed exclusively with the homozygous recessive parent to determine the F1​'s exact genotype (either homozygous dominant or heterozygous).

Test cross

A Test Cross is a critical tool in Mendelian genetics used to determine the unknown genotype of an individual that displays a dominant phenotype. It involves mating this unknown individual (which could be homozygous dominant, AA, or heterozygous, Aa) with a partner that is homozygous recessive (aa). The homozygous recessive parent is used because it can only contribute a recessive allele (a), serving as a "tester" to reveal the unknown parent's second allele. The results are conclusive: if all offspring display the dominant phenotype, the unknown parent is AA; however, if 50% of the offspring display the recessive phenotype, the unknown parent is definitively heterozygous (Aa), as it possessed and passed on the recessive allele to its progeny.

Reciprocal Cross

A cross between two sets, in one case, a male exhibiting the trait of interest is mated with a female that does not exhibit that trait, and in the other set, a female exhibiting the trait is crossed with a male lacking that trait. Its purpose is to observe the effect of parental sex on the inheritance of a trait. In autosomal inheritance transmission of trait is not affected by parental sex while reciprocal crosses given variable results in case of sex-inked inheritance, cytoplasmic inheritance and genomic imprinting.

It involves two breeding experiments:

  1. Cross 1 (Standard): A male exhibiting the trait of interest is crossed with a female that does not exhibit the trait.
  2. Cross 2 (Reciprocal): The sexes are reversed—a female exhibiting the trait is crossed with a male that lacks the trait.

The results of these two crosses are then compared to ascertain the genetic mechanism governing the trait:

Inheritance Pattern F1​ Offspring Results from Reciprocal Crosses Conclusion
Autosomal Inheritance The F1​ offspring phenotypes and ratios are identical in both the standard and reciprocal crosses. The gene is on a non-sex chromosome (autosome), and transmission is not affected by parental sex.
Non-Autosomal Inheritance The F1​ offspring phenotypes and/or ratios are different between the two reciprocal crosses. The trait is likely governed by Sex-Linked Inheritance (e.g., X-linked), Cytoplasmic Inheritance (maternal effect), or Genomic Imprinting.
