Understanding Survival and Mortality in Populations

Understanding Survival and Mortality in Populations

Studying survival and mortality in a population involves tracking groups of individuals to see how they fare over time. A common method is to follow a cohort, which is a group of same-aged organisms, from birth until their death. This allows researchers to understand how factors like age influence survival rates. Since a population often includes organisms of many different ages, it's also useful to look at age classes, which are groups of individuals all of the same age.

Information gathered from these studies is used to create a life table, which is a data-driven summary showing how many individuals are alive in each age class and the specific survival or death rates at those ages. This data can also be visualized as a survivorship curve, a graph that plots the number of surviving individuals over time.

There are two main types of life tables:

  • A cohort life table follows a specific group of individuals from birth to death. This is ideal for determining age-specific survival probabilities.
  • A static life table is different because it captures a "snapshot" of a population's age structure at a single point in time. This method is especially useful for organisms that live for a very long time, like elephants or trees, where it would be impractical to follow a single cohort throughout its entire lifespan. For example, a researcher named Adolf Murie used this approach when studying Dall Mountain sheep.

The Importance of Age Distribution

The composition of a population's age classes can tell us a lot about its health and future. While age data is often calculated annually, these studies frequently focus on females, as their reproductive capacity is typically the limiting factor for population growth. A few males can fertilize many females, but a lack of females directly limits the number of offspring produced.

The distribution of ages within a population is a strong indicator of its trajectory:

  • A population that is increasing usually has a large number of young individuals, which shows a high rate of reproduction.
  • A population that is decreasing tends to have very few young, indicating limited reproduction.

An imbalance in a population's age classes can have serious consequences. For instance, in an overfished population, the largest, oldest, and most fertile fish are often the first to be removed. If this is followed by a few years of poor reproduction, there will be no younger fish to mature and replace the ones that were caught, which can lead to a complete population collapse.

The Static Life Table: A Snapshot of Population Dynamics

A static life table offers a glimpse into a population's age structure at a specific point in time, using a single sample. We can explore this concept using the example of the North American beaver (Castor canadensis).

Historically, beavers were hunted to near extinction by the mid-19th century. However, 20th-century protection laws helped their populations rebound, so much so that they were sometimes considered a nuisance. In Newfoundland, Canada, trapping was used as a management tool. From 1964 to 1971, researchers collected beaver mandibles from trappers to determine the age of the animals. By analyzing the teeth, they could classify beavers into different age classes. For instance, a high number of teeth from 1-year-old beavers would suggest a large population in that age class, while a low number of teeth from 2-year-old beavers would indicate high mortality for the 1-year-old age class.

This data was used to construct a life table. The number of individuals alive at the start of an age class (e.g., a year) is denoted as nx. The number of individuals dying in a given age class, dx, can be calculated by subtracting the number alive at the start of the next age class from the number alive at the start of the current age class: dx=nx−nx+1. The Dall Mountain Sheep Life Table

Another classic example comes from a study on the Dall mountain sheep by Adolf Murie. In 1947, Edward Deevey organized Murie's data on 608 skulls into a life table (standardizing the numbers to represent a cohort of 1,000 individuals for easier comparison with other populations.

From this data, Deevey calculated several key population statistics:

1. Survivorship (lx):

Survivorship is the proportion of the original cohort that survives to a given age class. It is calculated as the number of individuals alive at the start of an age class (nx) divided by the total number of individuals in the original cohort (n0): lx=nx/n0 For instance, the survivorship to the sixth age class (5-6 years) is 734/1,000, or 0.734, meaning over three-quarters of the individuals survived to at least 5 years old.

2. Mortality Rate (qx):

The mortality rate within an age class is the proportion of individuals dying during that age class. It is calculated by dividing the number of deaths during an age class (dx) by the number of individuals alive at the beginning of that age class (nx): qx=dx/nx This measure helps determine how mortality changes with age. For the Dall mountain sheep, qx is high in the first age class, drops to a low level until about age 8, and then increases rapidly. Murie suggested that wolves might be responsible for the high mortality in both young and old sheep. However, a comparison with domestic sheep in New Zealand (where wolves are absent) showed similar mortality rates for all ages except lambs. This suggests that while Murie was correct about wolf predation impacting young sheep, old sheep would likely have died from other causes even without wolf predation. The data also revealed that female Dall sheep had a higher mortality rate than males.

Life Expectancy Calculations

3. Life Expectancy (ex):

Life expectancy is the average number of additional years an individual can expect to live at a certain age. Its calculation involves two intermediate steps:

  • Average Number of Individuals Alive (Lx): This is the average number of individuals alive during an age class, calculated as: Lx=(nx+nx+1)/2 For example, in the sixth age class, L6= (734+688)/2=711. This means that on average, 711 individuals are alive at any point during that age class.
  • Total Years Lived (Tx): This is the sum of the average number of individuals alive in an age class and all subsequent (older) age classes: Tx=∑x∞Lx For a 5-year-old sheep (age class 6), T6=L6+L7+L8+...=711+664+605.5+505+345.5+174+51+4.5+1.5=3,062.
  • Life Expectancy (ex): Finally, life expectancy is calculated by dividing the total years lived from a certain age (Tx) by the number of individuals alive at the start of that age class (nx): ex=Tx/nx For a 5-year-old sheep, e6=3,062/734=4.2. This indicates that on average, a 5-year-old sheep can expect to live for another 4.2 years.

Life tables allow ecologists to quantify key population features, such as life expectancy, survivorship patterns, and periods of high mortality. For the Dall sheep, the data showed that life expectancy actually increases after the first age class, highlighting the struggle for survival faced by newborns.

Limitations of Static Life Tables

Despite their value, static life tables have limitations. A primary assumption is that the birth rate is constant across all years. If, for example, more 2-year-old sheep were born in a particular year due to a favorable climate, a static life table might misinterpret their high number in a later sample as a higher mortality rate in the 2-year-old age class, even if the actual mortality rate was the same as other age classes. It is often difficult to get an independent estimate of birth rates for different age classes. Additionally, accurately assessing the age structure of a population can be challenging, even with methods like counting growth rings on horns, scales, or bones. Due to these difficulties, a cohort life table, which tracks a group of individuals from birth to death, is often considered a more accurate alternative.

Cohort Life Tables: Tracking a Generation from Cradle to Grave

Cohort life tables, unlike static ones, track the survival of an entire group of individuals (a cohort) from the time they are born until the last one dies.

To create a cohort life table, researchers must conduct population surveys frequently, especially for organisms with short lifespans, such as insects or annual plants. For these species, "age classes" might be measured in weeks or months, not years, and the study may last less than a year.

For longer-lived animals, like many birds or small mammals, a census conducted once or twice a year for up to a decade is sufficient. An example is the cohort life table for the American robin ( Turdus migratorius ), which was constructed by tracking banded nestlings throughout their lives
