AGE SPECIFIC FERTILITY

Population Ecology Overview

Population ecology is the study of how and why the size and structure of populations change over time. While a simple census provides a static snapshot, a deeper understanding requires a dynamic approach. Here we outlines a powerful method for this, which moves beyond simple counts to incorporate two critical pieces of demographic data: age-specific fertility and survivorship. By combining these, ecologists can not only assess the current state of a population but also predict its future.

The Building Blocks: Fertility and Survivorship

1. Age-specific fertility rate ($m_x$):

This metric quantifies the reproductive output of a population at different ages. It is defined as the average number of female offspring produced by a female of a specific age, x. For example, if a group of 100 three-year-old female beavers gives birth to 75 female babies, their $m_x$ value is 0.75. This indicates that, on average, each three-year-old female contributes 0.75 new female offspring to the next generation. This value highlights that fertility is not constant throughout an organism's life but varies with age.

2. Survivorship rate ($l_x$):

This metric describes the proportion of individuals from an initial cohort that survive to a specific age, x. It is a measure of mortality. For instance, if a study begins with a cohort of beavers and only 7.4% are still alive at age six, the $l_6$ value is 0.074. This shows the cumulative effect of mortality over time.

To understand the actual contribution of each age group to population growth, these two metrics are combined. The product, $l_x m_x$, represents the average number of offspring a female will produce at a specific age, after accounting for her probability of surviving to that age. This is a crucial concept because a highly fertile organism will not contribute to population growth if it dies before reaching reproductive age. The example of beavers shows that the most significant contributions come from intermediate ages (3-4 years old), where there is an optimal balance between high survival and peak fertility.

Calculating the Net Reproductive Rate ($R_0$)

The net reproductive rate, $R_0$, is the cornerstone of this analysis. It is calculated by summing the $l_x m_x$ values for all age classes.

$R_0 = \Sigma l_x m_x$

This number represents the total number of female offspring an average female is expected to produce over her entire lifetime, from birth to death. It is a single, powerful indicator of a population's health and future trajectory.

The value of $R_0$ serves as a clear diagnostic tool for population health:

  • $R_0$ > 1: The population is growing. Each female is, on average, more than replacing herself with new female offspring.
  • $R_0$ < 1: The population is shrinking. Females are not producing enough offspring to replace themselves, and the population is in decline.
  • $R_0$ = 1: The population is stable. Females are perfectly replacing themselves, and the population is in equilibrium.

The text provides two clear examples: a Newfoundland beaver population with an $R_0$ of 0.938 (less than 1), indicating a decline, and a barnacle population in Washington with an $R_0$ of 1.282 (greater than 1), indicating growth.

Predicting Future Population Size

Once $R_0$ is known, it can be used to predict the population size in the next generation. The relationship is simple and direct: the new population size ($N_{t+1}$) is the current population size ($N_t$) multiplied by $R_0$.

$N_{t+1} = N_t \times R_0$

For example, a population of 1,000 beavers with an $R_0$ of 1.1 would be expected to grow to 1,100 in the next generation. This predictive power is invaluable for conservation efforts and resource management.

Understanding Generation Time (T)

Generation time, denoted by T, is the final piece of this demographic puzzle. It is the average time that passes between a female's birth and the birth of her offspring. Essentially, it is the average age at which a female reproduces.

$T = \frac{\Sigma x l_x m_x}{R_0}$

Where:

  • x is the age class.
  • $l_x m_x$ is the contribution of that age class to the net reproductive rate.
  • The numerator, $\Sigma x l_x m_x$, is the sum of the products of age and the number of offspring produced at that age. This gives a weighted average of the age of reproduction.
  • The denominator, $R_0$, normalizes this sum to give the average time.

Generation time is crucial because it dictates the speed of population change. A population with a short generation time, like the barnacles (3.1 years), can grow or shrink much more rapidly than a population with a longer generation time, like the beavers (4.6 years).

Barnacle Population Study

A life table and age-specific fertility for the barnacle, Balanus glandula.

In 1970, Joe Connell's research on barnacles in Washington State revealed that, despite high mortality rates among young barnacles, the population was growing. His study focused on how different factors influenced barnacle population sizes, and he collected data on life expectancy and fertility.

Connell found that barnacles aged one year or older were highly fertile, with each female capable of producing up to 12,700 eggs. This high level of egg production resulted in a net reproductive rate ($R_0$) of 1.282, indicating a growing population. Most of the new barnacles were produced by adults between one and four years old, as older barnacles had poor survival rates. The average generation time (T) was approximately 3.1 years. Connell concluded that predation by snails of the genus Thais was the most significant factor affecting the barnacle population size.

Conclusion

By integrating data on age-specific fertility and survivorship, ecologists can calculate two fundamental metrics: the net reproductive rate ($R_0$) and generation time (T).

  • $R_0$ provides a clear and definitive diagnosis of a population's health (growing, shrinking, or stable).
  • T provides the timescale over which these changes occur.

Together, these metrics offer a complete and predictive picture of a population's dynamics. This approach is not merely academic; it is a vital tool for conservationists and resource managers who need to make informed decisions to protect and manage populations in a changing world.
