Introduction to Herbivory

Herbivory is the consumption of plants or algae. This feeding strategy is incredibly widespread and diverse, seen in a huge variety of animals. Large vertebrates like deer, goats, cows, rhinos, and elephants are well-known examples of plant-eaters. Humans, too, are herbivores when they eat plant-based foods.

The world of insects is home to over 360,000 herbivorous species, making them a significant group of plant-eaters. Many beetles, caterpillars, and grasshoppers are folivores, meaning they chew on plant leaves. In contrast, sucking bugs (Hemiptera) have a different approach. They use specialized mouthparts to pierce a plant's vascular system and draw out nutrients, typically from the phloem (which carries sugars) or, less often, the xylem (which transports water).

Unique and Niche Feeding Strategies

Beyond general chewing and sucking, some herbivores have evolved highly specific ways of feeding:

  • Gall Insects: Flies, aphids, and wasps can lay eggs on plant stems or leaf buds. This triggers the plant to form a tumor-like growth called a gall, which serves as a safe place for the larvae to live and feed.
  • Leaf Miners: These insects lay their eggs on leaves. Once hatched, the larvae feed between the leaf's surfaces, creating blister-like tunnels or "mines."
  • Subterranean Feeders: Some herbivores, like certain beetles, live underground, where they feed on a plant's roots.
  • Wood Borers: Bark beetles feed just beneath the bark of trees, while other beetles can burrow deep into the heartwood.

Categorizing Herbivores by Diet

Herbivores can also be classified by what specific plant parts they eat:

  • Folivores eat leaves.
  • Frugivores eat fruit (e.g., parrots).
  • Nectarivores consume only nectar.
  • Granivores eat only seeds.

Herbivores can also be classified by the range of their diet:

  • Monophagous: These species feed on only one plant species or a few closely related ones. Examples include pandas (which eat only bamboo) and koalas (which specialize in eucalyptus).
  • Polyphagous: These species have a much broader diet, eating a wide variety of plants, often from different families. Most large mammals are polyphagous, as are many insects like grasshoppers.

Given the large number of herbivore species, plant consumption can be deadly for plants, particularly for smaller ones. However, many plants have developed defenses and can regrow, especially larger species.

Defenses Against Herbivores

Even though plants seem like an easy food source because they can't move, we don't see herbivores eating as much plant material as one might expect. Three main hypotheses try to explain this:

1. Herbivore Self-Regulation

This idea suggests that herbivores intentionally limit their eating to avoid destroying their food source and to ensure a future supply. However, this is generally considered an unlikely explanation.

2. Predator and Parasite Control

Another theory is that predators and parasites keep herbivore populations in check. By preying on herbivores, they indirectly protect plants from being overconsumed. Evidence from various ecological studies supports this view.

3. Plant Defenses

A third, and very significant, reason is that plants are not defenseless. They have evolved various ways to protect themselves, including physical barriers like spines and tough cuticles, as well as a wide array of noxious chemicals.

These defenses can be one of two types:

  • Constitutive defenses are always present in the plant.
  • Induced defenses are only activated when a plant is under attack. This strategy allows plants to save energy by not producing defenses all the time, letting them use those resources for other functions.

It's important to note that the predator/parasite control and plant defense hypotheses are not mutually exclusive; both can contribute to limiting herbivory.

Mechanical Defenses

Plants' initial defense is mechanical. Thorns and spines deter large vertebrate herbivores, but not small invertebrates, which can simply chew around them. Many leaves have sticky hairs or trichomes that can trap and kill small insects, while other plants secrete sticky resins that gum up the mouthparts of insects.

Location-Based Defenses

  • Low-lying plants are generally more spiny than larger ones, as they are more vulnerable to being eaten by ground-level vertebrates.
  • Plants with only a few main growing points (like palms) often protect them with spines.
  • Evergreen species such as holly often develop spines because they are a primary food source for herbivores in winter.
  • African acacias have dense thorns on their outer parts, which are exposed to herbivores like giraffes, but fewer thorns on the inside. Young acacias are also particularly thorny. Some plants can even produce more thorns in response to being eaten.

Chemical Defenses

Plants have a secondary line of defense against herbivores in the form of powerful chemicals. These compounds, called secondary metabolites or secondary chemicals, are not essential for the plant's basic energy metabolism. These chemicals primarily deter herbivores and pathogens by being bad-smelling, bitter, or toxic. However, some have other functions; for example, the pleasant smell of flowers can attract pollinators.

The chemical defenses in many fruits also change as they ripen. Plants often produce a variety of these compounds, and many plant species share similar ones. Because of the sheer number of plant species, there is a vast diversity of these chemicals. Interestingly, many of these plant-based chemicals have medicinal properties that benefit humans, contributing to a global drug market worth over $1 trillion a year.

These compounds are grouped into three main classes based on their chemical structure:

  • Alkaloids: These contain nitrogen.
  • Phenolics: These do not contain nitrogen.
  • Terpenoids: These also do not contain nitrogen.

Alkaloids

Alkaloids are a class of over 12,000 nitrogen-containing molecules with a ring-like structure, known for their alkaline, or basic, nature. Common examples include caffeine, nicotine, and morphine. Many spices, like bay leaf and pepper, also contain alkaloids.

Alkaloids are often bitter and have an unpleasant smell, acting as a deterrent to herbivores. For instance, the alkaloid capsaicin in chili peppers creates a burning sensation. This deters mammals from eating the peppers, but birds, which help disperse seeds, are unaffected.

Some alkaloids are highly toxic. Atropine, found in deadly nightshade, is a powerful poison that can sicken or even kill animals by interfering with nerve signals. For example, in humans, it can dangerously increase heart rate by blocking the nerves that regulate it.

While not all alkaloids are lethal, they can still disrupt an animal's system. Some, like caffeine, are stimulants, while others, like morphine, act as depressants. Others can simply cause nausea by interfering with the nerves of the digestive system. An animal that survives an encounter with a toxic alkaloid is unlikely to eat that plant again.

Phenolics

Phenolic compounds, of which there are about 5,000, all share a chemical structure that includes a benzene ring. These compounds can be sorted into categories like flavonoids, tannins, and lignins.

Flavonoids are responsible for many of a plant's flavors and smells. They can act as either deterrents to herbivores or as attractants for pollinators. The flavors of things like chocolate and vanilla, for instance, come from flavonoids.

Tannins are large phenolic polymers. They get their name from their ability to bind with proteins in animal hides to make leather, a process called tanning. They are found in many plants, including grape skins (giving red wine its flavor) and tea leaves. Tannins can act as a defense against animals due to their bitter taste or toxic effects. When consumed in large amounts, they can also interfere with digestive enzymes. Tannins can be seen leaching from fallen leaves, giving some forest streams a coffee-like color.

Lignins are another type of large phenolic polymer. They are a primary component of plant cell walls, making up a significant portion of the dry weight of wood. By bonding with other cell wall components like cellulose, lignins strengthen plant cells, helping them withstand environmental stress and making wood very difficult for herbivores to digest.

Terpenoids

Terpenoids are a large and diverse group of over 25,000 secondary metabolites, all built from five-carbon isoprene units. They serve a variety of functions in plants. Many terpenoids are volatile, meaning they easily become airborne, which is why they are responsible for the distinct odors of many plants, such as the smell of mint from menthol. These odors can either attract pollinators or repel herbivores.

Terpenoids also give a strong flavor to plant tissues, with spices like cinnamon, cloves, and ginger being rich in them. Other well-known examples include camphor and the cannabinoids found in the cannabis plant.

Beyond their role in defense and attraction, terpenoids also have other uses. Carotenoids, a type of terpenoid, are responsible for the vibrant coloration of many plants, such as the orange color of carrots due to beta-carotene. While present in leaves throughout the year, their color is typically hidden by the green of chlorophyll; however, in the fall, as chlorophyll breaks down, the bright colors of the carotenoids become visible.

Many traditional herbal remedies use terpenoids, and they are currently being studied for potential medical applications.

Induced Defenses

Plants have a clever way of protecting themselves: they activate induced defenses in response to damage from herbivores. Research by Ian Kaplan and his team showed that when a plant's leaves are eaten, the plant produces more defenses in its leaves than in its roots. However, when the roots are attacked, the plant increases its defenses in both the roots and leaves equally. This suggests that the roots might be the site where these defenses are produced before being moved to the leaves.

In some cases, herbivore damage triggers the plant to release defense hormones like systemin and jasmonic acid. These compounds are volatile, meaning they can travel through the air and signal nearby plants to ramp up their own chemical defenses. This fascinating phenomenon has led to the concept of "talking plants," where plants communicate to warn each other of danger.

Chemical Defense Strategies

Plant chemical defenses are categorized as either quantitative or qualitative.

Quantitative Defenses

Quantitative defenses are substances consumed in large amounts that hinder an herbivore's ability to digest food and gain energy. These compounds, such as tannins and resins, can make up a significant portion of a leaf's dry weight, sometimes up to 60%. While not immediately toxic in small amounts, their effects are cumulative; the more a plant is eaten, the more difficult digestion becomes.

Paul Feeny's 1970 study on oaks showed that caterpillars eat more leaves in the spring when tannin levels are low, and their feeding decreases as the season progresses and tannin concentrations increase. This has led many caterpillar species to evolve as spring feeders, completing their life cycle before tannin levels get too high.

Qualitative Defenses

Qualitative defenses are highly toxic substances that are effective in very small doses, often making up less than 1% of a plant's dry weight. Examples include cyanogenic compounds and the potent poison atropine found in the deadly nightshade plant.

To avoid poisoning themselves, plants store these toxins in special glands or vacuoles. Atropine, for example, is so potent it was once used to dilate women's pupils for aesthetic reasons, giving the plant its name, belladonna, which means "beautiful lady." Interestingly, birds are immune to this poison, which allows them to safely disperse the plant's seeds.

Defense Strategies and Plant Apparency

The type of defense a plant uses is linked to its "apparency," or how easily herbivores can find it.

  • Apparent plants are large, long-lived, and easy to find (e.g., oak trees). They tend to rely on quantitative defenses because they have a long history of association with herbivores that can easily find them. These defenses are effective against both specialized (monophagous) and generalist (polyphagous) herbivores.
  • Unapparent plants are small, short-lived, and hard to find (e.e., weeds). They primarily use qualitative defenses to protect against polyphagous herbivores that might stumble upon them by chance. Quantitative poisons wouldn't work well for these plants because they would likely be consumed entirely before the poison had a cumulative effect.

Other Defense Hypotheses

  • Carbon-Nitrogen Balance Hypothesis: This theory suggests that a plant's defenses are determined by the balance of available carbon and nitrogen. In nitrogen-poor soil, plants prioritize using nitrogen for growth and produce more carbon-based defenses like tannins. In low-light environments where carbon is limited, they are more likely to produce nitrogen-rich toxins.
  • Optimal Defense Hypothesis: This idea proposes that a plant will allocate more defenses to parts that are essential and difficult to replace, like flowers and seeds. For example, the seeds of many edible fruits contain cyanogenic glycosides, protecting a vital part of the plant's reproductive cycle.

Additional Defense Strategies

In addition to their primary mechanical and chemical defenses, plants employ a variety of other strategies to protect themselves.

Herbivore-Mimicking Defenses

Some plants produce chemicals that disrupt herbivore behavior by mimicking their chemical messengers. For instance, semiochemicals are chemical signals that alter behavior. Some plants produce analogs of these chemicals to confuse herbivores, reducing their reproductive success.

  • Mimicking Hormones: Certain plants, like the houseplant Ageratum houstonianum, produce precocenes that prevent an insect from producing juvenile hormone. The lack of this hormone causes juvenile insects to prematurely molt into sterile adults, which limits their ability to reproduce. Other plants produce ecdysteroid-like compounds that mimic molting hormones, causing an insect to molt too early and often die.
  • Physical Mimicry: Some tropical vines, like those in the genus Passiflora, have physical structures that look like the eggs of the Heliconius butterfly. This tricks the butterflies, which are less likely to lay their eggs where others are already present, thus preventing their larvae from feeding on the vine.

Ecological and Behavioral Defenses

Plants have also developed defensive strategies that involve other organisms or specific behaviors:

  • Mutualism: Some plants form a mutualistic relationship with insects, like the bull's horn acacia tree with ants, where the ants defend the plant from herbivores.
  • Associational Resistance: Plants can grow near unpalatable plants that deter herbivores, a phenomenon known as associational resistance.
  • Abscission: Plants can selectively drop leaves that are heavily infested with immobile insects, such as leaf miners or aphids. This causes the insects to fall to the ground and prevents them from completing their life cycle.
  • Attracting Natural Enemies: When a plant is damaged by an herbivore, it can release specific volatile chemicals that attract the herbivore's natural predators or parasites. A study by Consuelo De Moraes and colleagues (1998) found that parasitic wasps (Cardiochiles nigriceps) were more attracted to tobacco and cotton plants damaged by their host, the tobacco budworm (Heliothis virescens), than to undamaged plants. The plants even produced different volatile compounds depending on which insect was attacking them, and the wasps could distinguish between them, showing a strong preference for plants damaged by their host. The study also proved the wasps were attracted to the plant's scent, not the caterpillar itself, as the wasps still visited the plants after the caterpillars and damaged leaves were removed.

Herbivore Co-evolution

Herbivores have developed five main ways to overcome a plant's defenses:

  1. Mechanical Adaptations: Many herbivores have specialized body parts for processing tough plant material. Vertebrates, for instance, have large, flat molars and strong jaw muscles to grind up plants. Ruminants have a complex, four-chambered stomach to help them digest tough fibers.
  2. Behavioral Adaptations: Herbivores use specific behaviors to avoid or lessen the effect of plant defenses. Some snip vegetation and wait for toxins to degrade before eating it. Small insects like caterpillars can chew through the vascular tissue that transports defenses, creating a safe feeding zone. Many herbivores also time their feeding to periods when a plant's chemical defenses are at their lowest concentration. Sucking bugs (Hemipterans) avoid many defenses altogether by feeding directly on the nutrient-rich sap.
  3. Physiological Adaptations (Enzymes and Detoxification): Herbivores produce a variety of enzymes to neutralize plant toxins. Many can create tannin-binding salivary proteins to inactivate the toxic effects of tannins. They may also have protective molecular mechanisms in their guts to prevent toxins from being absorbed or to pump them back out. The primary detoxification process is biotransformation, which converts secondary metabolites into harmless, easily excreted compounds. This process often begins with oxidation (using enzymes like mixed-function oxidases, or MFOs) followed by conjugation, where a harmful compound is combined with another molecule to make it inactive for excretion.
  4. Mutualistic Relationships: Many herbivores have a mutualistic relationship with gut microorganisms that help them digest plant material. For example, termites and wood-boring beetles rely on gut bacteria to break down wood. Ruminants also use bacteria to digest cellulose, and some insects even use plant toxins for their own defense. For instance, monarch butterflies store toxins from milkweed, making themselves poisonous to predators.
  5. Plant Manipulation: Some herbivores can manipulate the plant itself to reduce its defenses. For example, gall insects cause the plant to form a gall, which provides a safe, nutrient-rich environment for the insect to feed. The plant's defensive chemicals are often lower inside the gall, and the insect may even secrete its own chemicals to keep them low. Leaf miners live and feed between the leaf's surfaces, avoiding the outer parts where most defenses are concentrated.

The Evolutionary Arms Race

The relationship between plants and herbivores is a classic example of an evolutionary arms race. As plants develop new defenses, herbivores evolve new ways to overcome them, and vice versa. This process is known as co-evolution.

This arms race has led to the incredible diversity of both plant defenses and herbivore adaptations we see today. It's a major driver of evolutionary innovation and biodiversity.

How Much Plant Material Do Herbivores Consume?

Despite the vast number of herbivores, they actually consume a relatively small percentage of the total plant material produced each year. In most ecosystems, herbivores eat only about 5-10% of the total plant biomass. The rest is consumed by decomposers (bacteria and fungi) after the plant dies.

This low consumption rate is a testament to the effectiveness of plant defenses. It shows that while herbivory is a powerful selective pressure driving plant evolution, plants have been remarkably successful in defending themselves against being eaten.
