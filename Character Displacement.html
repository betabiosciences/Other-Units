CHARACTER DISPLACEMENT: EVOLUTIONARY RESPONSES TO COMPETITION

Definition and Evolutionary Basis

Character Displacement is an evolutionary phenomenon where two or more similar species, when co-occurring in the same geographic area (sympatry), exhibit greater differences in their characteristics compared to when they are found in isolation (allopatry). These divergent characteristics can manifest across various traits, including morphology, ecology, behavior, or physiology. This divergence in traits is driven by natural selection, which favors individuals with characteristics that reduce interspecific competition for shared, limited resources. The ultimate outcome is a minimization of competition through niche divergence.

Historical Context

The conceptual roots of character displacement can be traced back to Charles Darwin's "principle of divergence of character," which he proposed to explain how new species arise and diversify through competitive interactions. The term "character displacement" was formally coined by American entomologists William L. Brown and Edward O. Wilson in 1956. Their observations of morphological differences in ant species when co-occurring versus when found in isolation provided the empirical basis for this important concept.

Forms of Character Displacement

While character displacement can take several forms, the most relevant in the context of competitive exclusion and resource partitioning is:

  • Ecological Character Displacement (ECD): This form is caused by indirect (exploitative) competition between species for common resources, such as food or nesting sites. ECD typically results in a reduction of resource overlap through niche divergence. Other forms include Reproductive Character Displacement (RCD), driven by reproductive interference, and Agonistic Character Displacement (ACD), driven by aggressive interference.

Classic Examples and Observational Evidence

Darwin's Finches (Galapagos Islands):

A quintessential example of character displacement. On Daphne Major, the medium ground finch (Geospiza fortis) diverged significantly in beak size from the large ground finch (G. magnirostris) approximately 22 years after G. magnirostris arrived on the island and both species jointly and severely depleted the shared food supply. This observed evolutionary response to natural selection was one of the strongest recorded in 33 years of study, consistent with the high heritability of beak size. The distinct beak shapes and sizes allow these finch species to specialize on different food sources, thereby reducing competition.

Anole Lizards (Caribbean Islands):

Different Anolis species have evolved distinct morphological characteristics, such as differences in body size and limb length, which enable them to occupy different ecological niches and reduce competition.

Ant Species (Brown & Wilson):

The initial observations by Brown and Wilson highlighted that two species of ants exhibited more distinct morphological characteristics when co-occurring than when found in isolation, providing early evidence for the phenomenon.

Experimental Evidence for Character Displacement

Demonstrating character displacement requires fulfilling rigorous criteria, including ruling out patterns formed by chance and establishing a genetic basis for the observed trait differences.

Common Garden Experiments:

These experiments are crucial for demonstrating the genetic basis of trait differences. Individuals from sympatric and allopatric populations of a species are grown together in a common, controlled environment. If the trait differences persist under these uniform conditions, it strongly indicates a genetic basis rather than mere plastic responses to environmental variations. For example, Armbruster (1985) conducted common garden experiments with Dalechampia scandens, growing both rootstock and seeds from various populations. The observed divergence in reproductive traits (e.g., resin gland size) in the greenhouse matched those in their source populations, greatly strengthening the case for character displacement in this system.

Reciprocal Transplants:

This is another method used to test for evolved plasticity in response to competition, where individuals are moved between sympatric and allopatric sites.

Direct Tests of Natural Selection:

Recent studies have focused on documenting character displacement as it unfolds in real-time in the wild, identifying the role of directional selection during the initial stages of sympatry. For instance, in a study of two ecologically similar lizards (Anolis sagrei and A. cristatellus) experiencing novel contact, researchers documented directional selection leading to repeated trait divergence in the new community. This directly links short-timescale natural selection to broader patterns of character displacement at larger spatial and temporal scales.

"Natural Experiments":

Long-term field studies, such as those on Darwin's finches by Grant and Grant (2006), have provided compelling direct observations of character displacement occurring in natural populations.

Implications for Speciation and Community Structure

Character displacement is widely considered a major force in structuring ecological communities, influencing which species can coexist and how they interact. It plays a vital role in the generation of phenotypic diversity and is a key explanation for how new species arise and diversify. By driving evolutionary divergence and reducing competition, character displacement can enable species coexistence, leading to increased diversity and complexity within ecological communities.

Beyond interspecific interactions, competition can also drive divergence within a species, a process termed intraspecific character displacement. This occurs when selection acts to reduce competition among individuals of the same species, leading to "niche width expansion" or "ecological release". This process is frequently observed in isolated environments, such as islands or lakes, where a species encounters few heterospecific competitors. In such scenarios, the population can adaptively expand its niche to exploit previously underutilized resources, thereby reducing intraspecific competition. This can drive the evolution of novel resource-use phenotypes and potentially trigger adaptive radiation, where a single ancestral species rapidly diversifies into many new forms adapted to different niches. This broadens the scope of character displacement beyond just interspecific interactions, demonstrating its fundamental role in shaping diversity within species and in driving macroevolutionary processes like adaptive radiation. It shows that competitive pressures, whether interspecific or intraspecific, are powerful engines of evolutionary change.
