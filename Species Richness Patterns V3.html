SPECIES RICHNESS PATTERNS

1. The Species-Time Hypothesis

The species-time hypothesis suggests that biodiversity increases with time, proposing that temperate regions have fewer species than tropical regions primarily because temperate zones are geologically younger and have only recently recovered from severe climatic disruptions like glaciations (e.g., the last glacial maximum 18,000 years ago). These ice sheets and the resulting cold temperatures killed or displaced many species across North America and Europe.

Variations and Alternatives

The species-time hypothesis has several specific variations:

  1. Evolutionary Barriers: Tropical species may not have yet evolved the necessary traits, such as freezing tolerance, to exploit vacant niches in temperate zones. This is suggested by the observation that over half of flowering plant families lack temperate representatives.
  2. Migration Lag: Tropical species that could survive in temperate areas have not yet migrated back to the now-unglaciated northern areas they were driven out of during the ice ages.
  3. Evolutionary Speed Hypothesis: Evolution is hypothesized to be faster in the Tropics due to factors like high ambient temperatures, shorter generation times, and increased mutation rates. This suggests tropical areas serve as both a "cradle" for generating new species (taxa) and a "museum" for preserving existing ones.

These "time" hypotheses contrast with ecological hypotheses, such as the species-energy hypothesis, which posits that factors like temperature and water availability (ecological factors) primarily determine species richness, suggesting accumulation rates are similar in both realms.

Evidence Supporting the Species-Time Hypothesis

  • British Trees and Insects (H. John Birks, 1980): Birks found a significant correlation between the number of insect species on various British trees and the evolutionary age (time of colonization) of those trees in Britain. By using radiocarbon dating of pollen found in lake sediments, he determined that older tree species (present longer than recent colonists post-glaciation) supported more insect species, suggesting that time is important for accumulating biodiversity.
  • Glaciated vs. Unglaciated Lakes: Species richness is vastly different between similar-latitude lakes based on glaciation history. Lake Baikal (Siberia), an ancient, unglaciated temperate lake, harbors about 580 species of bottom-dwelling invertebrates, whereas Great Slave Lake (Northern Canada), where ice recently melted, contains only 4 species in the same zone.

Drawbacks and Conflicting Evidence

Ecologists recognize several limitations and contradictions regarding the species-time hypothesis:

  1. Vertebrate Parasite Richness (Bush et al., 1990): This study found that habitat mattered most, not taxon age. Aquatic vertebrates had more helminth parasite species than terrestrial ones. Contrary to the hypothesis, the oldest lineage (fish) had fewer parasite species than younger lineages (aquatic mammals or birds).
  2. Limited Marine Applicability: While terrestrial species may be slow to redistribute (due to barriers like the English Channel) following glaciations, there's no clear reason why marine organisms couldn't shift distributions easily in response to climate changes. Yet, the polar-equatorial gradient of species richness still exists in marine habitats.
  3. Ectotherm vs. Endotherm Limitation: The evolutionary speed variation relies on high temperature accelerating generation times and mutation rates, which primarily affects ectotherms (cold-blooded animals). However, endotherms (warm-blooded animals) also show pronounced latitudinal species richness gradients, limiting the general applicability of the evolutionary speed idea.
  4. Conflicting Speciation Rate Data:
    • Tropical Plants (Wright et al., 2006): Found that tropical plant species had more than twice the rate of molecular evolution compared to their closely related temperate counterparts, supporting the "cradle" idea of faster tropical evolution.
    • New World Birds and Mammals (Weir and Schluter, 2007): Found that the highest speciation and extinction rates occurred at high latitudes and declined toward the Tropics. This directly conflicts with the notion that high tropical diversity is a result of high speciation rates in the Tropics.

2. The Species-Area Hypothesis

The species-area hypothesis posits that larger geographic areas support a greater number of species than smaller areas.

Core Mechanisms and Supporting Evidence

The hypothesis relies on two main mechanisms:

  1. Population Size: Larger areas can maintain larger populations, which are inherently more resistant to extinction.
  2. Habitat Range: Larger areas tend to encompass a wider range of habitats, which promotes increased speciation rates.

Empirical Support

  • British Trees and Insects (Strong, 1974): Donald Strong found that the richness of insect species on various British trees was directly correlated with the geographic area over which the tree species was distributed.
  • Introduced Species Data: This hypothesis is supported by data on introduced species, such as apple trees in Britain and various global crop species (e.g., sugarcane). The species richness of insects and pests on these plants correlates better with the area planted than with the time since their introduction. Strong argued that this evidence countered the species-time hypothesis, as some relatively new (low time) but widespread (large area) species supported high insect diversity.

Application to Tropical Diversity and Criticisms

Tropical Areas

The Tropics possess the largest contiguous land area with symmetrically similar climates, unlike the Northern and Southern Hemispheres where similar climates are separated by temperate or polar zones. This large, unified area has been proposed as a reason for the Tropics' exceptional species richness, supporting the species-area hypothesis.

Criticisms and Drawbacks

The hypothesis struggles to explain certain patterns of species distribution:

  1. Vast Landmasses: If increased richness is solely tied to increased area, then regions like the vast contiguous landmass of Asia should harbor more species than they currently do.
  2. Large Biomes with Low Richness: The tundra is a geographically large land biome, yet it exhibits very low species richness.
  3. Marine Systems: The open ocean is the largest marine system by volume, but it has fewer species compared to tropical surface waters, which occupy a relatively smaller volume. This suggests that factors other than volume/area are dominant in some ecosystems.

3. The Species-Energy Hypothesis

The species-energy hypothesis proposes that the amount of available energy determines the species richness of a community.

Mechanism and Supporting Evidence

The central argument is:

  • High levels of solar energy and water abundance drive high plant productivity (total weight of plant material produced).
  • Increased plant productivity supports a greater number of herbivores, which in turn supports more predator, parasite, and scavenger species, leading to higher overall species richness.

Empirical Support: Evapotranspiration

  • David Currie and colleagues (1987) found that tree species richness in North America is best predicted by the evapotranspiration rate.
  • Evapotranspiration—the rate water moves into the atmosphere through soil evaporation and plant transpiration—is directly influenced by solar energy and ground water.
  • Consequently, tree richness is highest in the hot, moist southeast United States, where evapotranspiration is high. Tree richness is low in the desert southwest because, despite high temperatures, low rainfall and water availability limit productivity.

Limitations and Contradictions

While the hypothesis offers a strong correlation, there are several exceptions:

  1. Low Productivity, High Richness: Some tropical marine areas (e.g., the southeast Pacific off Colombia and Ecuador) have low productivity but surprisingly high species richness.
  2. High Productivity, Low Richness:
    • The sub-Antarctic Ocean has high productivity but low species richness.
    • Estuarine areas are highly productive but low in species, likely because the fluctuating freshwater and saltwater conditions create a stressful environment.
    • Polluted lakes with fertilizer have high productivity but low species richness.
  3. Tropical vs. Temperate Forests: Despite the fact that temperate and tropical forests have similar productivity (with temperate forests sometimes being higher), tropical forests exhibit much higher species richness. This contradicts the idea that richness scales directly with local productivity.
  4. Mechanism Ambiguity: It remains unclear why increased productivity should lead to an increase in species richness rather than simply leading to high population densities of just a few species.
  5. Continental Comparisons: While the pattern holds for tree richness within North America, it fails when comparing continents: Temperate forests of eastern Asia (with 729 tree species) support substantially more species than the climatically similar temperate forests of North America (253 species) or Europe (124 species). This suggests that factors like evolutionary history and opportunity for species invasion are also highly important.

Ultimately, ecologists recognize that while the species-time, species-area, and species-energy hypotheses offer explanations for the pole-equator gradient in species richness, local species numbers are also influenced by other phenomena, such as disturbance and natural enemies.

4. The Intermediate Disturbance Hypothesis

The Intermediate Disturbance Hypothesis (IDH) suggests that local species richness is highest in communities that experience intermediate levels of disturbance (e.g., fires or floods).

Connell's Rationale (1978)

Joe Connell proposed that disturbance frequency influences species richness through the balance of competition and colonization:

  • Low Disturbance Rate: Species richness is low. Competitively dominant species (typically K-selected species, which are good competitors existing near the environment's carrying capacity, K) outcompete and exclude all other species.
  • High Disturbance Rate: Species richness is low. Disturbances continually drive species extinct, permitting only good colonists (typically r-selected species, which have high population growth rates and disperse well) to persist by moving in from adjacent habitats.
  • Intermediate Disturbance Rate: Species richness is highest. The frequency of disturbance is just right to prevent competitive exclusion (preventing K-selected species from dominating) while not being so severe as to eliminate all but the best colonists. This allows for the coexistence of both colonists (r-selected) and competitors (K-selected).

Corollaries (Variations)

The IDH also suggests that richness is greatest in areas where disturbances are:

  1. Of intermediate size (creating open habitat patches without obliterating all species).
  2. Occurring at intermediate time intervals after an event (allowing both colonists and competitors to co-occur before competitors replace the colonists).

Experimental and Observational Evidence

Marine Intertidal Boulders (Sousa, 1979)

Wayne Sousa tested the IDH on algal and animal species richness on intertidal boulders, where wave action acts as the disturbance:

Boulder Size Disturbance Frequency Mean Species Count Species Type Conclusion
Small High (easily moved) 1.7 species Primarily r-selected Most species crushed; low richness.
Large Low (rarely moved) 2.5 species Primarily K-selected Competitive dominants supplanted others; low richness.
Intermediate Intermediate 3.7 species Mix of r- and K-selected Highest richness.

Japanese Beech Forests (Hiura, 1995)

Tsutomu Hiura studied species diversity in Japanese beech forests across a latitudinal gradient and found that locations with an intermediate mean windstorm interval (disturbance frequency) had the highest species diversity. Windstorms create light gaps, preventing the dominance of superior competitors while not being frequent enough to drive many tree species extinct.

Additional Support

The IDH is supported by:

  • Coral reefs that maintain high diversity in areas frequently disturbed by hurricanes.
  • The fact that the richest tropical forests often occur where storm disturbance is common.
  • The observation that some of the richest plant communities in the southeastern United States are found on army bombing ranges, which experience high but sporadic rates of disturbance.

5. The natural enemies hypothesis

The natural enemies hypothesis suggests that the presence and action of natural enemies (predators, herbivores, etc.) increases local species richness by reducing the population density of dominant prey species. This decrease in prey density lessens inter-prey competition, thereby allowing more prey species to coexist.

Paine's Keystone Species Experiment

Robert Paine (1966) provided key experimental evidence in the intertidal zone of the U.S. northwest coast (Mukkaw Bay and Tatoosh Island, Washington).

  • The Predator: The starfish, Pisaster, is a common predator that primarily preys on the mussel (Mytilus californianus) but also consumes barnacles, limpets, and other organisms.
  • The Experiment: When Paine removed Pisaster from a section of the shore, species richness dramatically decreased from 15 to 8 species.
  • The Result: The prey species Mytilus californianus (mussel) and the gooseneck barnacle (Pollicipes polymerus) were released from predation pressure. They increased in number and monopolized the space, consequently crowding out many other species. In unmanipulated areas, Pisaster kept the dominant species in check.
  • Keystone Species: Because the removal of Pisaster had such a drastic effect on the entire community structure—an effect not seen when any other single species was removed—Paine termed Pisaster a keystone species, essential for maintaining species diversity.

This phenomenon, where enemies prevent competitive exclusion, was also historically noted by Charles Darwin, who observed that more grass species coexisted in areas grazed by sheep or rabbits than in ungrazed areas.

Species Richness Across Scales

Ecologists recognize that different processes influence species richness at different spatial scales:

  • Continental Scale: Factors like evolutionary speed (time) and species-area effects may be most important.
  • Regional Scale: The time since the last glaciation significantly impacts richness patterns.
  • Landscape Scale: Disturbance frequency (Intermediate Disturbance Hypothesis) becomes a key factor.
  • Small (Local) Scales: Biotic interactions, such as the action of natural enemies, exert a strong influence.

Species richness at any location is likely determined by the simultaneous interaction of these factors, and global climate change is predicted to drastically alter local richness as temperature and precipitation regimes shift.
