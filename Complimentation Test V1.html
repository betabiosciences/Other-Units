Complementation Test

Introduction

The complementation test , or complementation analysis , is an analytical procedure used to determine whether two independent recessive mutations that produce a similar phenotype occur at the same locus (are allelic) or at different loci (are nonallelic) . The original technique was devised by Drosophila geneticist Edward B. Lewis .

Procedure

The test is carried out by crossing two parents, each homozygous for a different recessive mutation, to produce heterozygous offspring (F1 generation). The phenotype of the F1 offspring is then analyzed.

Outcomes

Outcome Interpretation Complementation Status
Wild-type phenotype Mutations are in separate genes (nonallelic). Complementation Occurs
Mutant phenotype Mutations affect the same gene (allelic). Lack of Complementation

Case 1: Complementation Occurs (Different Loci)

Parents: Homozygous mutant for gene A but wild-type for gene B (ab+ a b+) crossed with homozygous mutant for gene B but wild-type for gene A (a+b a+b).

F1 Genotype: ab+ a+b(Heterozygous for both genes).

Result: The offspring inherit one mutant allele and one wild-type allele at each locus. The presence of the wild-type allele (b+) from one parent complements the mutation at the A locus (a), and vice versa. This produces the necessary normal products for both genes, resulting in the wild-type phenotype .

Case 2: Lack of Complementation (Same Locus/Allelic)

Parents: Two mutations (ma and mb) are different mutant alleles of the same gene (Gene M).

F1 Genotype: The offspring are effectively homozygous for the mutant gene M, inheriting two different mutant alleles (e.g., ma/mb).

Result: Since the mutations affect the same gene, no normal product of that gene is produced. The mutations fail to complement each other, resulting in the mutant phenotype .

Complementation Groups

Complementation analysis can be used to screen any number of individual mutations that result in the same phenotype (a heterogeneous trait ).

  • All mutations determined to be present in any single gene are said to fall into the same complementation group .
  • Mutations within the same group will not complement each other.
  • Mutations in one complementation group will complement mutations in all other groups.

By classifying mutations into these groups, it's possible to predict the total number of genes involved in the determination of that trait.

Summary

If two mutations failed to complement

  • They are alleles of the same gene
  • They are allelic to each other
  • They belong to the same complementation group

If two mutations complements each other

  • They are alleles of different genes
  • They are not allele to each other
  • They belong to different complementation group

1. Mutations at Different Loci (Nonallelic)

Genotype: The parents are aa b+b+ and a+a+ bb.

Offspring Genotype: The offspring are aa+ bb+. This means they have one wild-type allele ( a+ ) and one mutant allele ( a ) for the first gene, and one wild-type allele ( b+ ) and one mutant allele ( b ) for the second gene.

Phenotype: The wild-type alleles "complement" the mutant alleles, meaning the offspring has at least one functional copy of each gene.

Result: The offspring exhibits the wild-type phenotype . Complementation has occurred.

2. Mutations at the Same Locus (Allelic)

Genotype: Both mutations affect the same gene.

Parents: The parent on the left is homozygous for the recessive mutant allele a (aa ). The parent on the right is homozygous for a different recessive mutant allele b(bb).

Crucially: In this scenario, a and b represent two different mutations within the same gene .

Offspring Genotype: The offspring inherits one mutant chromosome from each parent, resulting in the heterozygous genotype ab. Since both alleles are mutant forms of the same gene, the organism lacks a functional copy of that essential gene, the necessary protein or function cannot be produced.

Phenotype: The two mutant alleles do not "complement" each other.

Result: The offspring exhibits the mutant phenotype . This result indicates the mutations are allelic (part of the same complementation group). Lack of complementation has occurred.

Example 1: Wingless Mutation in Drosophila

Case 1. All offspring develop normal wings

Interpretation: The two recessive mutations are in separate genes and are not alleles of one another. Following the cross, all F1 flies are heterozygous for both genes. Since each mutation is in a separate gene and each F1 fly is heterozygous at both loci, the normal products of both genes are produced (by the one normal copy of each gene), and wings develop. Under such circumstances, the genes complement one another in restoration of the wild-type phenotype, and complementation is said to occur because the two mutations are in different genes.

Case 2. All offspring fail to develop wings.

Interpretation: The two mutations affect the same gene and are alleles of one another. Complementation does not occur. Since the two mutations affect the same gene, the F1 flies are homozygous for the two mutant alleles (the ma allele and the mb allele). No normal product of the gene is produced, and in the absence of this essential product, wings do not form.

Complementation analysis of alternative outcomes of two wingless mutations in Drosophila (ma and mb). In case 1, the mutations are not alleles of the same gene, while in case 2, the mutations are alleles of the same gene.

Example 2: Eye Colour mutants in Drosophila

In fruit flies, white is an X-linked mutation that produces white eyes instead of the red eyes found in wild type flies. Apricot is an X-linked recessive mutation that produces light orange-coloured eyes. When the complementation test is applied to white and apricot mutations, all of the heterozygous offspring have light coloured eyes, demonstrating that white and apricot are produced by mutations that occur at the same locus and are allelic (occur at the same locus) . If they were non-allelic independent assorting genes all progeny would have been red eyed due to complementation.

If an apricot-eyed female is mated with a white-eyed male, the female offspring are all heterozygous and have mutant light-coloured eyes. Thus, apricot and white are functional alleles, they do not complement. Similarly, buff, cherry and eosin eye colour also not showing complementation. We can conclude that white, apricot, buff, cherry and eosin belong to same allelic/complementation group.

Only Prune and Ruby complement with white eye mutant which means, prune and Ruby must be at different locus (non-allelic) to white. Prune and Eosin also do not complement with each other means they both are belonging to the same. Thus, there are two complementation groups.

Cis and Trans Arrangement

The complementation test is specifically a test for function in the trans arrangement of two mutations.

Trans Arrangement (Complementation Test):

  • The two mutant alleles are on opposite homologous chromosomes .
  • If a wild-type phenotype is observed, the mutations are complementing (nonallelic). If a mutant phenotype is observed, they are failing to complement (allelic).

Cis Arrangement (Control):

  • The two mutant alleles are on the same homologous chromosome
  • This arrangement always places one complete set of wild-type alleles on the second chromosome. Assuming the mutations are fully recessive, this configuration should always produce a wild-type phenotype , regardless of whether the mutations are in the same or different genes. The cis arrangement is typically used as a control to confirm the mutations are recessive and that a single wild-type copy is sufficient for normal function.
