Cohesin: The Chromosome Guardian

Cohesin is a multi-protein complex essential for proper cell division. Its primary function is to hold sister chromatids together after DNA replication, ensuring accurate chromosome segregation during mitosis and meiosis.

Beyond chromosome cohesion, cohesin plays roles in DNA repair and gene regulation through chromatin loop formation. Dysregulation can lead to aneuploidy, developmental disorders, and cancer.

Molecular Structure

The cohesin complex forms a ring-like structure composed of four core subunits:

  • SMC1 - Structural Maintenance of Chromosomes protein 1
    SMC1 forms one half of the ATPase motor that drives cohesin's DNA binding activity. It dimerizes with SMC3 to form the core of the cohesin ring.
  • SMC3 - Structural Maintenance of Chromosomes protein 3
    SMC3 pairs with SMC1 to create the V-shaped hinge that can open and close the cohesin ring. It's essential for cohesin's dynamic loading and unloading.
  • RAD21 (Scc1) - Kleisin subunit
    RAD21 bridges SMC1 and SMC3, completing the ring structure. It's cleaved by separase during anaphase to release sister chromatid cohesion.
  • SCC3 (SA/STAG) - Regulatory subunit
    SCC3 binds RAD21 and interacts with other regulatory proteins. Different variants (SA1, SA2 in humans) determine cohesin's localization and function.

The ring structure can topologically entrap DNA strands, allowing cohesin to physically link sister chromatids after DNA replication.

G1 Phase

Cohesin is loaded onto chromatin by the Scc2-Scc4 (NIPBL-MAU2 in humans) complex.

Binding is dynamic during this phase, with Wapl promoting unloading. Continuous activity of loading machinery is required to maintain cohesin on chromatin.

S Phase

Cohesion between sister chromatids is established as DNA replicates. Cohesin entraps the newly synthesized chromatids.

Cohesin stability increases at this stage, transitioning from dynamic to stable binding.

G2 Phase

Cohesin maintains sister chromatid cohesion, preparing chromosomes for mitosis.

The complex remains stably bound to chromatin throughout this phase.

Prophase

Most cohesin is removed from chromosome arms via the "prophase pathway," but it remains at centromeres.

This partial removal helps condense chromosomes while maintaining crucial centromeric cohesion.

Metaphase-Anaphase Transition

At the metaphase-anaphase transition, the protease separase cleaves the RAD21 subunit.

This releases centromeric cohesin, allowing sister chromatids to separate. Separase is kept inactive by securin until this point.

Telophase

Cohesin is reloaded onto chromatin for the next cell cycle.

This marks the beginning of a new cycle of cohesin loading and regulation.

Regulation Mechanisms

Cohesin's activity is controlled through several precise mechanisms:

Loading

Scc2-Scc4 (NIPBL-MAU2 in humans) loads cohesin onto DNA, requiring ATP hydrolysis. This occurs primarily in G1 and telophase.

Establishment

During S phase, cohesin establishes stable cohesion as DNA is replicated. This process converts dynamically bound cohesin into stably bound cohesive complexes.

Release/Removal

Wapl and other factors promote cohesin release from chromatin, especially during G1 and prophase. This allows for chromatin reorganization.

Cleavage

At anaphase, separase cleaves RAD21, leading to chromatid separation. This irreversible step ensures proper chromosome segregation.

Phosphorylation

Phosphorylation and proteolysis of Scc2 regulate cohesin loading and activity, ensuring cell cycle specificity. Various kinases target cohesin subunits at different stages.

Additional Roles

While best known for its role in sister chromatid cohesion, cohesin has several other important functions:

Gene Regulation

Cohesin mediates long-range chromatin interactions (chromatin looping), influencing gene expression and maintaining pluripotency. It works with CTCF to organize chromatin architecture.

DNA Repair

Cohesin helps repair double-strand DNA breaks by holding sister chromatids together as templates for repair. It's recruited to damage sites and facilitates homologous recombination.

Developmental Regulation

Mutations in cohesin or its regulators cause developmental disorders (cohesinopathies) like Cornelia de Lange syndrome, highlighting its importance beyond cell division.
