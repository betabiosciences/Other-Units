Non-Mendelian Inheritance - Allelic Interactions

Introduction

Mendel's initial work established the concept that hereditary factors (genes) come in alternate forms (alleles), specifically identifying a dominant and a recessive version.

However, subsequent research has demonstrated that this model is an oversimplification. Genes frequently exist in more than two allelic forms (multiple alleles), and these alleles can interact in complex ways to influence the observable characteristics (phenotype). These findings, which include various types of allelic interactions (like incomplete dominance or codominance) and gene interactions (like epistasis), represent extensions of basic Mendelian ideas.

1. Codominance

Codominance is a genetic condition where a heterozygote simultaneously and fully expresses the traits of both alleles. Neither allele is dominant over the other; instead, their characteristics appear independently. The resulting phenotype is a combination of both homozygous forms, rather than a blend.

A classic example is the MN blood group in humans. This system is determined by a gene with two codominant alleles, one for the M antigen (LM) and one for the N antigen (LN).

  • LM LM (Homozygous): Produces only M antigens on red blood cells.
  • LN LN (Homozygous): Produces only N antigens.
  • LM LN (Heterozygous): Produces both M and N antigens.

In a cross between two heterozygotes (LM LM and LN LN), the phenotypic ratio is 1:2:1 (1 M blood type : 2 MN blood type : 1 N blood type).

2. Incomplete Dominance (Part 1)

Dominance can be understood in regard to how the phenotype of the hetero zygote relates to the phenotypes of the homozygotes. Flower color potentially ranges from red to white. One homozygous genotype, A1A1, produces red pigment, resulting in red flowers; another, A2A2, produces no pigment, resulting in white flowers. Where the heterozygote falls in the range of phenotypes determines the type of dominance. If the heterozygote (A1A2) produces the same amount of pigment as the A1A1 homozygote, resulting in red, then the A1 allele displays complete dominance over the A2 allele; that is, red is dominant over white. If, on the other hand, the heterozygote produces no pigment resulting in flowers with the same color as the A2A2 homozygote (white), then the A2 allele is completely dominant, and white is dominant over red. When the heterozygote falls in between the phenotypes of the two homozygotes, dominance is incomplete. With incomplete dominance, the heterozygote need not be exactly intermediate between the two homozygotes; it might be a slightly lighter shade of red or a slightly pink shade of white. As long as the hetero zygote's phenotype can be differentiated and falls within the range of the two homozygotes, dominance is incomplete.

2. Incomplete Dominance (Part 2)

Incomplete dominance is also exhibited in the fruit color of eggplant. When a homozygous plant that produces purple fruit (PP) is crossed with a homozygous plant that produces white fruit (pp), all the heterozygous F1 (Pp) produce violet fruit. When the F1 are crossed with each other, 1/4 of the F2 are purple (PP), 1/2 are violet (Pp), and 1/4 are white (pp),. Note that this 1 : 2 : 1 ratio is different from the 3 : 1 ratio that we would observe if eggplant fruit color exhibited complete dominance. Another example of incomplete dominance is feather color in chickens. A cross between a homozygous black chicken and a homozygous white chicken produces F1 chickens that are gray. If these gray F1 are intercrossed, they produce F2 birds in a ratio of 1 black : 2 gray : 1 white.

2. Incomplete Dominance (Part 3)

A 1 : 2 : 1 phenotypic ratio arises in the progeny of a cross between two parents heterozygous for a character that exhibits incomplete dominance (Aa × Aa). The genotypic ratio among these progeny also is 1 : 2 : 1. When a trait displays incomplete dominance, the genotypic ratios and phenotypic ratios of the offspring are the same, because each genotype has its own phenotype. The important thing to remember about dominance is that it affects the phenotype that genes produce but not the way in which genes are inherited.

3. Multiple Allelism

The classical Mendelian concept of a gene having only two allelic states was expanded after the discovery of genes with three or more alleles.

a. Rabbit Coat Color

A classic example is the gene for rabbit coat color (c), which has four alleles:

  • c+ (wild-type, most common)
  • cch (chinchilla)
  • ch (himalayan)
  • c (albino, or colorless)

The wild-type allele is commonly symbolized by a superscript plus sign (+). The other alleles are mutants. Genetics often names a gene after a recessive mutant allele (like c for albino), which usually produces the most abnormal phenotype. If a mutant allele is dominant (like the T gene for a short tail in mice), the gene is named after the associated dominant phenotype.

c+ >cch> ch>c

The different phenotypes are caused by four different alleles of the c gene. In case of multiple alleles, the number of different genotypes possible among diploid organisms is a function of the number of alleles that exist for any given gene. If 'n' is the number of alleles of a gene, the number of different genotypes possible is n(n+1)/2.

3. Multiple Allelism - Human ABO Blood Types

b. Human ABO Blood Types

The A, B, AB, and O human blood types are controlled by the gene I, which has three alleles:

  • IA (specifies the A antigen)
  • IB (specifies the B antigen)
  • i (specifies no antigen)

This system demonstrates:

  • Codominance: The IA and IB alleles are equally expressed in the IA IB heterozygote (Type AB blood).
  • Recessiveness: The i allele is recessive to both IA and IB.

The presence of these three alleles at high frequencies means the I gene is polymorphic ("having many forms").

ABO Blood Types - Details

We can represent the dominance relations among the ABO alleles as follows: IA > i, I B > i, IA = I B. The IA and the I B alleles are dominant over i and are codominant with each other; the AB phenotype is due to the presence of an IA allele and an I B allele, which results in the production of A and B antigens on red blood cells. A person with genotype ii produces neither antigen and has blood-type O.

The six common genotypes at this locus and their phenotypes. The body produces antibodies against any foreign antigens. A person with blood-type A produces anti-B antibodies because the B antigen is foreign to that person. A person with blood-type B produces anti-A antibodies, and a person with blood-type AB produces neither anti-A nor anti-B antibodies because neither A nor B antigen is foreign to that person. A person with blood-type O possesses no A or B antigens; consequently, that person produces both anti-A antibodies and antiB antibodies. The presence of antibodies against foreign ABO antigens means that successful blood transfusions are possible only between people with certain compatible blood types.

Rh Blood Group (Part 1)

Discovered by Landsteiner and wiener (1940). The Rh antigens are located on two Rhesus proteins-RhD and RhCE- encoded by two highly polymorphic genes, RhD and RhCE, and are expressed only RBCs. RhD protein carries the D antigen and the RhCE protein carries CE antigens in various combinations (ce, Ce, cE, CE).

The classification of Rh- positive and Rh- negative individuals is determined by the presence or absence of the D antigen on the surface of RBCs. If D antigen is present on a person's RBCs, the person is Rh-positive.

Each biological parent donates one of their two Rh alleles to their child. A person who is Rh- negative (genotype of Rh/Rh) can only pass Rh allele to son or daughter. A person who is Rh-positive (genotype could be either Rh/Rh or Rh²/Rh) can pass either Rh+ or Rh allele to son or daughter. Rh-positive phenotype is dominant over Rh-negative phenotype. If both of a child's parents are Rh negative, the child will definitely be Rh-negative. Otherwise the child may be Rh positive or Rh-negative, depending on the parent's specific genotypes.

Rh Blood Group (Part 2)

The Rh antigen is of particular significance when Rh-negative mothers give birth to Rh positive babies. The foetal and maternal bloods are normally kept separate across the placenta and so the Rh-negative mother is not usually exposed to the Rh antigen of the foetus during the pregnancy. However, during the delivery of the first child, there is a possibility of exposure of the maternal blood to small amounts of the Rh-positive blood from the foetus. In such cases, the mother starts preparing antibodies against the Rh antigen in her blood. However, this does not occur always because the exposure may be minimal and because Rh negative women vary in their sensitivity to the Rh antigen. If the woman does produce antibodies against the Rh antigen, these antibodies can cross the placenta in subsequent pregnancies and cause haemolysis of the Rh-positive red blood cells of the foetus. Therefore, the baby could be born anaemic with a condition called erythroblastosis fetalis (or haemolytic disease of the newborn).

Rh Blood Group (Part 3)

Erythroblastosis fetalis can be prevented by injecting the Rh-negative mother with an antibody prepared against the Rh antigen (called anti-Rh antibodies or Rhogam) within 72 hours after the birth of each Rh-positive baby. This is a type of passive immunization in which the injected antibodies inactivate the Rh antigens and thus prevent the mother from becoming actively immunized to them.

4. Pseudoallelism (Part 1)

Pseudoalleles are closely linked genes located near one another on the same chromosome that have similar or related functions. They were initially misidentified as different allelic forms of the same gene, which is why they received the prefix "pseudo-" (meaning false or deceptive).

Key Features and Characteristics

1. Close Linkage and Inheritance:

  • Pseudoalleles are situated so close together on a chromosome that they are genetically linked and are nearly always inherited together as a single unit.
  • Because they are so close, they appear to act as a single gene controlling a trait.

2. Shared Function and Character Effect:

  • They have similar functions and affect the same character or trait.
  • This similarity in effect is what causes them to be confused with true alleles.

4. Pseudoallelism (Part 2)

3. Crossing Over/Recombination:

  • Unlike true alleles, which are different forms of the same gene and thus cannot recombine internally, pseudoalleles are two separate genes.
  • Therefore, crossing over (recombination) can occur between them, although rarely due to their close proximity. This distinguishes them as non-allelic genes.

4. Origin Hypothesis:

  • A prevailing hypothesis suggests that pseudoalleles arise from gene duplication events.
  • Following duplication, the two copies (the pseudoalleles) remain closely linked and can evolve new, but related, functions.

Example- The red eye color of Drosophila:

  • Mutants like white and apricot affect the same character (eye pigmentation).
  • Based on their effect on the same character, they were first considered to be allelic (different forms of the same gene).
  • However, detailed genetic analysis showed that recombination (crossing over) could occur between them, demonstrating they are non-allelic---meaning they are two separate, but closely linked, genes (pseudoalleles).

A major real-world example of a pseudoallelic gene complex is the Hox cluster, a set of genes crucial for regulating body plan development in animals.

In summary, pseudoalleles represent a complex genetic state where two functionally related, extremely close genes mimic the behavior of a single, highly mutable gene, with the key difference being the potential for rare recombination to occur between them.
