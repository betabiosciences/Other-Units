Understanding Survivorship Curves

Ecologists use survivorship curves to quickly compare the survival patterns of different organisms. These graphs plot the number of surviving individuals against their age. The data can come from either a static life table (a snapshot in time) or a cohort life table (following a group from birth).

The number of surviving individuals, often represented as nx, is typically shown on a logarithmic scale. This is because a log scale helps ecologists focus on the rate of change in a population, rather than just the absolute numbers.

For example, a population that starts with 1,000 individuals and loses 500 in a year has a log decrease of log₁₀1,000−log₁₀500=3.0−2.7=0.3.

Similarly, a population starting with 100 individuals that loses 50 has a log decrease of log₁₀100−log₁₀50=2.0−1.7=0.3.

In both cases, the rate of change is the same (0.3 per year), even though the number of individuals lost is different. Plotting the data on a log scale ensures that the rate of change of one species' curve can be easily compared to another's, regardless of their initial population size.

For instance, the survivorship curve for beavers on a log scale is nearly a diagonal line, indicating a uniform death rate throughout their lives.

Population Structure and Survivorship of the North American Beaver

The population of North American beavers, like many species, is age-structured, meaning it is composed of individuals of varying ages. Ecologists study these populations using tools like life tables, which track a cohort—a group of same-aged individuals—from birth to death to understand survival and mortality patterns.

The provided graph illustrates a survivorship curve for the North American beaver, which plots the number of surviving individuals (n_x) on a logarithmic scale against their age in years. The curve's shape reveals key insights into the beaver's life history:

Initial High Mortality:

The curve shows a relatively steep decline during the first couple of years of life (ages 0-2). This indicates a high mortality rate for young beavers, likely due to factors such as predation or lack of resources. This initial pattern is characteristic of a Type III survivorship curve, where many offspring are produced to compensate for high early-life losses.

Stable Adult Survivorship:

After the initial vulnerable period, the curve flattens out significantly for the majority of the beaver's adult life (approximately ages 2 to 9). This indicates a consistent, low mortality rate. This stage of the curve is a defining feature of a Type II survivorship curve, where the probability of death is relatively constant throughout the lifespan.

Increased Mortality in Old Age:

The curve begins to drop more sharply again after age 9, indicating an increase in the death rate as beavers reach the end of their lifespan. This is a common pattern for many species, reflecting the effects of aging, disease, and reduced physical capability.

In conclusion, while the beaver's survivorship curve shows an initial period of high mortality, its overall pattern—especially during the long adult phase—is best characterized as a Type II survivorship curve. This suggests that once a beaver survives its vulnerable youth, it has a consistent chance of survival throughout its prime years, which is a common survival strategy for species that provide significant parental care and have fewer offspring than those with a true Type III curve.

Types of Survivorship Curves

Ecologists use three main types of survivorship curves to represent the patterns of survival within a population.

Type I Survivorship

In a Type I curve, the death rate is low for young individuals and increases significantly as organisms get older, making them more susceptible to sickness and predators. Species with this pattern, such as many large mammals including humans, generally produce few offspring but invest heavily in their care. The data for Dall mountain sheep in Denali National Park is a classic example of a Type I curve. There's a slight decline in survival for young lambs, but then the curve flattens out, showing high survival through about age 7 or 8 before the death rate rises sharply in older sheep. This data supports the observation that mortality is highest among the very young and the very old.

Type III Survivorship

At the opposite end is a Type III curve, where there is a very high death rate for juveniles, and the curve flattens out for the few individuals who survive early life. This pattern is common in many fish, marine invertebrates, insects, and plants. These species often produce a large number of offspring, but most die before reaching maturity. For example, barnacles release many juveniles that are lost at sea, but those that find a suitable rock face to attach to are likely to survive and thrive. The survivorship curve for the barnacle Balanus glandula shows massive mortality in the first year of life. Similarly, many insects and plants lay a large number of eggs or release hundreds of seeds, most of which do not survive.

Type II Survivorship

A Type II curve represents a middle ground, characterized by a fairly consistent death rate throughout an organism's life. This pattern is seen in many birds, small mammals, reptiles, and some annual plants. A beaver population, for instance, would be a good example of a Type II survivorship curve.

It is important to remember that these are general models, and few populations fit them perfectly. Human activities, such as harvesting specific age groups, can also impact a species' survivorship curve.

Static vs. Cohort Life Tables

When studying populations, ecologists use different methods to collect data. A cohort life table tracks a group of individuals born around the same time throughout their entire lives. In contrast, a static life table takes a snapshot of a population at a single point in time, recording the number of individuals in each age class.

Comparing the accuracy of these two methods is difficult for most species, but it is possible for humans using historical birth and death records.

For example, a cohort survivorship curve for the British peerage born in 1800 can be compared to a static curve of a hypothetical English population in 1812. The static curve in this case would be less accurate because it ignores environmental changes over time. The 72-year-olds in the 1812 static curve would have been born in 1750, a time when medical care and diet were less advanced than they were for the people who reached age 70 in 1882. Therefore, the static table's prediction for how many 70-year-olds would be alive in 1882 would be based on outdated data.

In the natural world, environmental factors like climate and food supply vary from year to year, which is why a cohort life table is always more accurate than a static one. However, because it is often impractical and resource-intensive to track long-lived populations from birth to death, static life tables are a quicker and more affordable way to get a general idea of a population's age structure.

Conclusion

In conclusion, survivorship curves are essential tools for ecologists to visualize and compare the survival strategies of different species. These curves, whether derived from static or more accurate cohort life tables, reveal fundamental patterns in how mortality rates change with age. The three primary models—Type I (low juvenile mortality, high old-age mortality), Type III (high juvenile mortality, low adult mortality), and Type II (constant mortality rate)—each correspond to a distinct life history strategy. While few species fit these idealized curves perfectly, they provide a valuable framework for understanding population dynamics. The North American beaver serves as a compelling example of a species that primarily exhibits a Type II pattern, characterized by a consistent survival rate during its prime adult years after an initially high-risk juvenile phase. This highlights that many species' curves are a blend of these ideal types, reflecting complex interactions between an organism's life history, its environment, and factors like parental care and resource availability.
