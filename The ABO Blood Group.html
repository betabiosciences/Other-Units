THE ABO BLOOD GROUP - THE GENETICS BEHIND IT

Introduction

The ABO blood group system is arguably the most crucial classification system for human blood, first discovered by Austrian physician Karl Landsteiner in 1900. Its significance lies in its direct role in safe blood transfusions, as mismatches can trigger life-threatening immune reactions. The classification into four main types—A, B, AB, and O—is based on the presence or absence of specific antigens (A and B) on the surface of red blood cells (RBCs) and the corresponding natural antibodies in the plasma.

The Genetic Basis

The ABO blood type is determined by a single gene, the ABO gene, located on chromosome 9. This genetic locus exhibits a classical example of multiple alleles and codominance, key concepts in genetics.

Alleles

There are three main alternative forms (alleles) of the gene: IA, IB, and i (sometimes denoted IO).

Gene Function

The IA and IB alleles encode functional glycosyltransferase enzymes. These enzymes add a specific sugar molecule to a precursor molecule (the H antigen) already present on the red cell surface:

  • The IA allele produces the A transferase, which creates the A antigen.
  • The IB allele produces the B transferase, which creates the B antigen.

Recessiveness

The i (or IO) allele is recessive; it results from a small deletion in the gene that produces a non-functional enzyme. Consequently, it leaves the H antigen unmodified, resulting in the O blood type (lacking both A and B antigens).

Dominance and Codominance

  • IA and IB are dominant over IO. Therefore, genotypes IA IA or IA IO result in type A blood, and IB IB or IB IO result in type B blood.
  • IA and IB are codominant with each other. A person inheriting both (IA IB) expresses both transferases, resulting in red cells with both A and B antigens, classified as type AB blood.

Understanding these genetic principles—multiple alleles, dominance, and codominance—is essential for predicting inheritance patterns, ensuring transfusion safety, and exploring the system's evolutionary and clinical significance.

Multiple Allelism

Another multiple-allele system is at the locus for the ABO blood group. This locus determines ABO blood type and, like the MN locus, encodes antigens on red blood cells. The three common alleles for the ABO blood group locus are: IA, which encodes the A antigen; IB, which encodes the B antigen; and i, which encodes no antigen (O). We can represent the dominance relations among the ABO alleles as follows: IA > i, IB > i, IA = IB. Both the IA and the IB alleles are dominant over i and are codominant with each other; the AB phenotype is due to the presence of an IA allele and an IB allele, which results in the production of A and B antigens on red blood cells. A person with genotype ii produces neither antigen and has blood type O.

Antibodies are produced against any foreign antigens. For instance, a person having blood-type A produces anti-B antibodies, because the B antigen is foreign. A person having blood-type B produces anti-A antibodies, and someone having blood-type AB produces neither anti A nor anti-B antibodies, because neither A nor B antigen is foreign. A person having blood-type O possesses no A or B antigens; consequently, that person produces both anti-A antibodies and anti-B antibodies. The presence of antibodies against foreign ABO antigens means that successful blood transfusions are possible only between persons with certain compatible blood types.

Terminal Sugar that Determines ABO Blood Group

The ABO blood type is determined by the terminal sugars on glycoproteins on red blood cells: type A has N-acetylgalactosamine, type B has galactose, and type O has no additional terminal sugar beyond the basic precursor structure. Individuals with type AB blood have both N-acetylgalactosamine and galactose on their cells. The "O" blood type results from a lack of the enzymes that add the A or B sugars, while the H antigen is the precursor that both A and B antigens are built upon.

Blood Type Terminal Sugar
Type A The terminal sugar is N-acetylgalactosamine.
Type B The terminal sugar is galactose.
Type O There is no additional terminal sugar added to the H antigen precursor.
Type AB Both N-acetylgalactosamine and galactose are present as terminal sugars on different oligosaccharide chains.

How It Works

Precursor: All ABO antigens are built on a common precursor structure called the H antigen, which is formed by adding a fucose sugar to the end of an oligosaccharide chain.

Enzymes: The ABO blood group is determined by genes that code for enzymes called glycosyltransferases.

Type A: The A gene produces an enzyme that adds N-acetylgalactosamine to the H antigen precursor.

Type B: The B gene produces an enzyme that adds galactose to the H antigen precursor.

Type O: Individuals with type O blood have two non-functional alleles, so they don't produce either the A or B enzyme. This means the H antigen is the final form on their red blood cells, as there is no enzyme to modify it.

The Bombay Phenotype (hh Blood Group) - Recessive Epistasis

Recessive epistasis is a type of gene interaction where the homozygous recessive genotype at one gene locus (the epistatic gene) masks or suppresses the expression of alleles (both dominant and recessive) at a second, different gene locus (the hypostatic gene).

Feature Description
Epistatic Gene The gene that does the masking. The masking effect is only seen when this gene is in the homozygous recessive state (e.g., aa).
Hypostatic Gene The gene that is masked (hidden) by the epistatic gene. The alleles of this gene (e.g., B and b) will only be expressed if the epistatic gene has at least one dominant allele (e.g., A_).
F2 Phenotypic Ratio 9:3:4 (instead of 9:3:3:1). The two classes that include the homozygous recessive epistatic genotype are combined, resulting in the "4" portion of the ratio.

The Bombay phenotype (or hh blood group, also known as Oh) is an extremely rare blood type first discovered in 1952 in Bombay (now Mumbai), India, by Dr. Y.M. Bhende.

Key Characteristics and Genetics

  • Antigen Absence: Individuals with this phenotype lack the H antigen on their red blood cells (RBCs) and in their secretions.
  • ABO Group Inexpression: The H antigen is the precursor molecule required to form the A and B antigens of the ABO blood group system. Since the H antigen is absent, the A and B antigens also cannot be formed or expressed, regardless of the individual's ABO genotype.
  • Antibodies Present: Their serum contains naturally occurring anti-A, anti-B, and a powerful anti-H antibody. This anti-H is what distinguishes it from the common O blood type.
  • Genotype: The condition is caused by inheriting two recessive alleles (hh) of the FUT1 gene, which codes for the enzyme that produces the H antigen. This is an example of epistasis, where one gene (FUT1) controls the expression of another (ABO).
  • Rarity: Its global prevalence is about 1 in a million, but it is higher in certain communities, particularly in India (up to 1 in 10,000 in Mumbai), often due to consanguineous marriages.

Clinical Significance and Transfusion Challenge

  • Transfusion Restriction: This is the most critical aspect. Bombay phenotype individuals can only receive blood from another person with the Bombay phenotype (Oh).
  • Fatal Reaction Risk: If they receive blood from any other ABO type (A, B, AB, or even normal O, which has the H antigen), their strong anti-H antibody will react, causing a severe and potentially fatal acute hemolytic transfusion reaction.
  • "Universal Donor" Paradox: While they can donate to any recipient in the ABO system (because their blood lacks all A, B, and H antigens), they are not universal recipients.
  • Misdiagnosis: The phenotype is often misidentified as Type O in routine forward blood typing because their RBCs do not react to anti-A or anti-B. The difference is only revealed during reverse grouping or anti-H testing, which is crucial for safe transfusion.

Genetics of the Broader ABO System

The Bombay phenotype is a special case of a genetic concept called epistasis within the ABO blood group system.

1. Multiple Alleles and Codominance

  • Gene Locus: The ABO blood group is controlled by a single gene located on Chromosome 9.
  • Multiple Alleles: There are three common alleles for this gene: IA, IB, and i (often written as IO).
  • Dominance Relationships:
    • IA and IB are Codominant to each other. If both are present (genotype IA IB), both A and B antigens are expressed, resulting in Type AB blood.
    • IA and IB are Dominant over the i allele, which is Recessive. The i allele does not produce any functional antigen.

2. The Role of the H Antigen and Epistasis

  • The FUT1 Gene: The H antigen, which the Bombay phenotype lacks, is produced by a separate gene called FUT1, located on Chromosome 19.
  • Precursor Molecule: The H antigen is a precursor molecule. The enzymes produced by the IA and IB alleles of the ABO gene must attach to this H antigen to create the final A or B antigens, respectively.
  • Epistasis in Bombay Phenotype: The Bombay phenotype is an example of recessive epistasis.
    • The genotype is hh (recessive at the FUT1 locus), meaning no H antigen is produced.
    • Even if the person inherits IA or IB alleles (e.g., a genotype of IA IB hh), the A and B enzymes have no H precursor to modify.
    • Therefore, the expression of the ABO genes is masked by the hh genotype, and the blood type is functionally O (specifically Oh).

In this example, the alleles at the ABO locus are hypostatic to the recessive h allele.

The Bombay phenotype provides us with a good opportunity for considering how epistasis often arises when genes affect a series of steps in a biochemical pathway. The ABO antigens are produced in a multistep biochemical pathway , which depends on enzymes that make H and on other enzymes that convert H into the A or B anti gen. Note that blood-type O may arise in one of two ways: (1) from failure to add a terminal sugar to compound H (genotype H_ ii) or (2) from failure to produce compound H (genotype hh_). Many cases of epistasis arise in this way. A gene (such as h) that has an effect on an early step in a biochemical pathway will be epistatic to genes (such as IA and IB) that affect subsequent steps, because the effects of the genes in a later step depend on the product of the earlier reaction.

Codominance in the ABO Blood Group System

The ABO blood group system is a classic and medically important example of codominance in human genetics, which occurs specifically in individuals with Type AB blood.

Codominance is a relationship between two alleles of a single gene where neither allele is dominant or recessive to the other. Consequently, when both alleles are present in the genotype, both traits are fully and equally expressed in the phenotype.

The ABO Example

The ABO blood type is governed by a single gene with three common alleles: IA, IB, and i (sometimes written IO). The codominance occurs between the IA and IB alleles:

Allele Encoded Product Dominance Status
IA alpha-N-acetylgalactosaminyl transferase (A antigen) Codominant with IB; Dominant to i
IB alpha-D-galactosyl transferase (B antigen) Codominant with IA; Dominant to i
i Non-functional enzyme (No antigen produced) Recessive to IA and IB

When an individual inherits the IA allele from one parent and the IB allele from the other, their genotype is IA IB.

  • Since IA and IB are codominant, the body simultaneously produces both functional enzymes (A transferase and B transferase).
  • These two enzymes then modify the precursor H antigen on the red blood cell surface, resulting in the production of both A and B antigens.
  • The resulting phenotype is Type AB blood, where both the A and B antigens are fully expressed side-by-side on the red blood cell surface.

Summary

The ABO blood group system, discovered by Karl Landsteiner, is critical for safe blood transfusions and provides a classical model for complex genetic principles. The blood type (A, B, AB, or O) is determined by the ABO gene on chromosome 9, which features multiple alleles (IA, IB, and i). IA and IB encode functional enzymes (glycosyltransferases) that add terminal sugars (A or B antigens) to the H antigen precursor. Codominance is seen in Type AB blood (genotype IA IB), where both A and B antigens are fully expressed, while IA and IB are both dominant over the recessive I allele, which produces no functional antigen (Type O blood). Furthermore, the rare Bombay phenotype demonstrates recessive epistasis, where the homozygous recessive genotype (hh) at the separate FUT1 gene locus (on chromosome 19) prevents the formation of the H antigen precursor, thereby masking the expression of the ABO alleles and resulting in a functional Type O blood (Oh).
